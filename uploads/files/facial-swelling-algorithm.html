Safety

Red Flags — Airway Compromise & Life-Threatening Causes

Airway assessment is the priority. Any facial swelling that could track to the floor of mouth, neck or retropharynx is a potential airway emergency.
Ludwig's angina Bilateral submandibular swelling + raised floor of mouth + drooling + stridor → 999. Rapidly fatal airway emergency from dental infection.
Stridor / drooling Any stridor, drooling, or inability to swallow own saliva with facial/neck swelling → 999. Impending airway compromise.
Angio-oedema Sudden soft-tissue swelling of lip/tongue/throat + urticaria / allergen exposure → 999. Give IM adrenaline 0.5 mg (1:1000) if anaphylaxis.
Orbital cellulitis Periorbital swelling + proptosis + restricted/painful eye movements + fever → 999 ophthalmology. Intracranial extension risk.
Cavernous sinus thrombosis Bilateral periorbital swelling + headache + fever + chemosis → 999. Complication of facial/sinus infection.
Rapidly expanding neck mass Increasing swelling tracking towards neck + trismus + fever → same-day hospital (deep space neck infection — descending necrotising mediastinitis risk)
Hard fixed painless mass Non-tender, fixed, progressive facial/parotid swelling ± weight loss → 2WW head and neck cancer
Trismus (jaw won't open) Limited mouth opening + swelling + fever → peritonsillar abscess or masseteric space abscess → same-day hospital
Ludwig's angina has a mortality of up to 50% without immediate surgical airway — it spreads rapidly along fascial planes from a mandibular molar infection to the submandibular, sublingual, and submental spaces simultaneously. The airway can close within hours. Orbital cellulitis carries intracranial extension risk (cavernous sinus thrombosis, subdural empyema) in 5–10% of cases — proptosis and restricted eye movement are the defining features distinguishing orbital from pre-septal (periorbital) cellulitis. Angioedema with laryngeal involvement has a 5–10 minute window before airway occlusion — IM adrenaline must be given without delay.

Diagnose

History — Location, Onset & Associated Features

Location
Periorbital (orbital/pre-septal cellulitis, allergy), cheek (dental abscess — upper/lower molar), angle of jaw (parotid gland), submandibular (lymph node, submandibular gland, dental), midline (thyroglossal cyst), neck (lymph nodes)
Onset
Sudden (minutes/hours — angio-oedema, allergy, insect sting) vs hours–days (dental abscess, cellulitis, salivary duct obstruction) vs weeks–months (malignancy, sarcoid, Sjögren's)
Pain
Severe + dental history → dental abscess. Colicky at mealtimes → salivary duct calculus (submandibular most common). Painless progressive → malignancy, Sjögren's parotid enlargement
Dental history
Toothache, recent dental work, broken/carious tooth. Dental cause is the single most common cause of facial swelling — ask specifically about lower molar pain.
Systemic symptoms
Fever (infection, malignancy), weight loss, night sweats (lymphoma), bilateral parotid enlargement (Sjögren's, sarcoid, HIV, alcoholic parotitis), dry eyes/mouth (Sjögren's)
Allergen exposure
New food, drug (ACE inhibitor — most common cause of drug-induced angio-oedema), insect sting, latex. ACE inhibitor angio-oedema can occur after years of use.
ACE inhibitor-induced angio-oedema affects 0.1–0.7% of patients and can occur at any point during treatment — years after starting. The mechanism is bradykinin accumulation (not IgE-mediated), so adrenaline, antihistamines, and steroids are less effective. Treatment is stopping the ACE inhibitor — switch to ARB (lower risk). Salivary duct calculi (sialolithiasis) are typically submandibular (85%) due to the saliva being more alkaline and mucous. Mealtime swelling that reduces between meals is pathognomonic — the salivary gland swells on stimulation, then drains partially at rest. Bilateral parotid enlargement is the classic presentation of Sjögren's syndrome (xerostomia + keratoconjunctivitis sicca + parotid enlargement).

Diagnose

Differential Diagnosis by Anatomical Zone

Dental / odontogenic (most common)
Periapical abscess (from carious tooth), periodontal abscess, pericoronal abscess (wisdom tooth). Localised to one side, fluctuant or indurated. Dental X-ray diagnostic.
Salivary gland
Parotid: mumps (bilateral, painful — check vaccination status), parotitis, parotid calculus, pleomorphic adenoma (benign, slow-growing), parotid malignancy. Submandibular: calculus, sialadenitis.
Lymph nodes
Reactive (URTI, dental, scalp infection, EBV), suppurative lymphadenitis (bacterial), TB lymphadenitis (painless, matted), lymphoma (rubbery, non-tender, persistent). Location guides drainage area.
Inflammatory / autoimmune
Sjögren's (bilateral parotid, dry eyes/mouth), sarcoidosis (bilateral parotid + hilar lymphadenopathy — Heerfordt's syndrome), GCA (temporal swelling, temporal artery tenderness, age >50)
Skin and soft tissue
Cellulitis (dental origin, insect bite, trauma — erythematous, warm, tender, spreading), sebaceous cyst (punctum visible), lipoma (soft, lobulated, non-tender), dermoid cyst
Vascular / congenital
Arteriovenous malformation (AVM — bluish discolouration, pulsatile), haemangioma, branchial cyst (lateral neck, young adults), thyroglossal cyst (midline, moves on tongue protrusion)
Dental infections account for 60–70% of facial swelling presentations in primary care — the anatomical relationship between roots of the lower molars and the submandibular space explains why seemingly minor dental infections can rapidly become life-threatening. Pleomorphic adenoma is the most common benign parotid tumour and grows slowly over years — it requires surgical excision (parotidectomy) as it has 5% malignant transformation risk if untreated. Heerfordt's syndrome (uveitis + parotid enlargement + facial nerve palsy + fever) is a specific sarcoidosis presentation — bilateral parotid swelling with uveitis is virtually pathognomonic and should prompt ACE level and CXR.

Diagnose

Targeted Examination

Airway assessment first
Stridor, drooling, voice change (muffled/hot potato = peritonsillar abscess), trismus, inability to open mouth — any of these → 999 before completing examination
Swelling characteristics
Fluctuant (pus — abscess, ready to drain) vs indurated/woody (cellulitis, tumour) vs compressible (lymphoedema, vascular) vs pulsatile (AVM). Transilluminates (cyst) vs does not (solid tumour).
Skin / surface
Erythema + warmth → infection. Fixed to skin or underlying bone → malignancy. Mobile → benign. Punctum visible → sebaceous cyst. Parotid duct (Stensen's duct) — milk pus by massaging gland.
Mouth and teeth
Dental inspection — carious/broken tooth, gum swelling, pus at gum margin, trismus (mouth opening <35 mm = significant), floor of mouth elevation (Ludwig's)
Lymph nodes (full chain)
Cervical (anterior/posterior), submandibular, submental, pre/post-auricular, supraclavicular. Tenderness/warmth → reactive/infective. Hard fixed → malignancy. Rubbery matted → lymphoma/TB.
Eyes
Proptosis, restricted eye movement, chemosis, visual acuity — distinguish orbital from pre-septal cellulitis. Dry eyes (Schirmer's test — Sjögren's)
Facial nerve
Facial nerve palsy with parotid swelling → parotid malignancy until proven otherwise. Test all branches: forehead, eye closure, nasolabial fold, mouth movement.
Facial nerve palsy in association with a parotid mass is a sinister combination — malignant parotid tumours invade the facial nerve as they expand through the gland. The presence of facial weakness with parotid swelling mandates urgent 2WW head and neck cancer referral. Trismus (limited mouth opening <35 mm) indicates involvement of the masticator space — this can be caused by pericoronitis, masseteric space abscess, or temporal muscle involvement and impairs anaesthetic airway management. Milking the parotid duct (massaging the gland while visualising Stensen's duct orifice adjacent to the upper second molar) is a simple bedside test — pus emerging confirms parotitis.

Diagnose

Investigations

Bloods — directed
FBC (leukocytosis — infection; lymphocytosis — EBV/viral) · CRP / ESR · Mumps serology (IgM — if bilateral parotid, unvaccinated) · Monospot / EBV IgM (glandular fever)
Dental imaging
Dental panoramic tomography (OPG) — identifies periapical abscess, dental caries, impacted wisdom teeth, jaw lesions. Request via dentistry or hospital OPG unit.
Salivary gland
Ultrasound salivary gland — identifies calculi (>90% sensitivity), abscess, benign vs cystic vs solid mass. First-line imaging for parotid / submandibular swelling.
Suspected malignancy
USS + FNAC (fine needle aspiration cytology) — organised via 2WW head and neck cancer pathway. MRI parotid is gold standard for soft tissue characterisation (via ENT).
Autoimmune screen
Anti-Ro (SSA) + Anti-La (SSB) (Sjögren's — 70% sensitive) · ANA · ACE level (sarcoidosis) · CXR (bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy — sarcoid)
NOT routinely
CT face for uncomplicated dental abscess — reserve CT for deep space infections, orbital complications, or when MRI/USS non-diagnostic. Radiation dose to lenses.
Ultrasound is the first-line investigation for salivary gland pathology — it is readily available, radiation-free, and identifies calculi (which are radiolucent on plain X-ray) with 90% sensitivity. The dental panoramic radiograph (OPG) is the most informative single dental image and should be arranged promptly for all suspected odontogenic facial swelling — it identifies the causative tooth, any jaw cysts, and bony involvement. Anti-Ro/La antibodies are positive in 70% of Sjögren's syndrome — a negative result does not exclude the diagnosis (seronegative Sjögren's exists). Salivary gland biopsy (minor salivary gland lip biopsy) is the gold standard for Sjögren's diagnosis when serology is negative.

Refer

Referral Pathways

999
Ludwig's angina, stridor/drooling, anaphylaxis/angio-oedema with throat involvement, orbital cellulitis with proptosis
Same-day hospital
Spreading dental infection tracking to neck, trismus + fever + swelling, cavernous sinus thrombosis signs, periorbital swelling with restricted eye movement
2WW head & neck
Unexplained parotid lump ≥3 weeks, parotid swelling + facial nerve palsy, hard fixed facial mass, cervical lymphadenopathy ≥3 weeks with no infective cause, suspected mucosal head and neck cancer
Urgent dental
Same-day or next-day emergency dental: dental abscess with swelling (needs incision and drainage ± extraction). NHS 111 dental helpline for emergency dental access if no registered dentist.
ENT — routine
Recurrent parotitis, salivary duct calculus (sialendoscopy — minimally invasive stone removal), submandibular gland excision if recurrent sialadenitis
Rheumatology
Suspected Sjögren's syndrome (anti-Ro/La positive or characteristic features) · suspected sarcoidosis with systemic features
The 2WW head and neck cancer pathway covers parotid tumours (including the 20% of parotid tumours that are malignant) — any unexplained parotid mass lasting more than 3 weeks warrants referral regardless of soft consistency. Sialendoscopy (endoscopic removal of salivary duct calculi) has a 90% success rate and avoids gland excision — this is now available in most ENT centres. Facial nerve palsy with parotid swelling has a 60% positive predictive value for parotid malignancy in published series — it must be treated as cancer until proven otherwise and investigated within the 2WW pathway.

Treat

GP-Initiated Treatment

Dental abscess — mild
Amoxicillin 500 mg TDS × 5 days
Antibiotics only as adjunct to dental treatment — refer same-day emergency dental. NICE guidance: antibiotics without dental treatment are ineffective and should not be prescribed without concurrent dental referral. Metronidazole 400 mg TDS if penicillin allergy.
Facial cellulitis
Co-amoxiclav 625 mg TDS × 5–7 days
Covers streptococcal and anaerobic organisms. Flucloxacillin 500 mg QDS if staphylococcal (post-trauma). Cefalexin 500 mg QDS if penicillin allergy (non-anaphylactic). Review at 48 hrs — admit if spreading or fever not settling.
ACE inhibitor angio-oedema
Stop ACE inhibitor immediately
Switch to ARB (angiotensin receptor blocker — lower angio-oedema risk). No antihistamines or steroids (bradykinin-mediated, not IgE). Icatibant (bradykinin antagonist) available if recurrent/severe — specialist initiated. Warn about future episodes.
Allergic angio-oedemaIM adrenaline 0.5 mg (1:1000) if systemic → 999. Chlorphenamine 10 mg IV/IM + hydrocortisone 200 mg IV in hospital. EpiPen prescribed on discharge. Allergy clinic referral.
Salivary calculusEncourage salivary flow — hydration, lemon drops, massage gland. Analgesia (ibuprofen 400 mg TDS). ENT referral for sialendoscopy. Antibiotics only if acute sialadenitis (co-amoxiclav).
Sjögren's / sarcoidArtificial tears (hypromellose) + saliva substitutes (Biotène). Hydroxychloroquine 200–400 mg OD (rheumatology initiated) for systemic Sjögren's. Oral prednisolone for sarcoid — specialist directed.
NICE guidance (NICE CKS Dental Abscess, 2023) emphasises that antibiotics without dental treatment are ineffective for dental abscesses — the pus collection must be drained. Prescribing antibiotics alone delays definitive treatment and contributes to antimicrobial resistance. ACE inhibitor angio-oedema is bradykinin-mediated (not IgE) — antihistamines and steroids are largely ineffective, which is why this presentation can be dangerous. Icatibant (a bradykinin B2 receptor antagonist) is used in severe ACE inhibitor angio-oedema in secondary care. The switch from ACE inhibitor to ARB reduces but does not eliminate recurrence risk (ARBs cause angio-oedema in ~0.5% vs 1–3% for ACEi).

Lifestyle

Prevention & Self-Management

Dental hygiene Twice-daily brushing (2 minutes), daily interdental cleaning, annual dental check-up. Dental abscess is 100% preventable with good oral hygiene. Fluoride toothpaste (1450 ppm for adults).
Dental registration Register with NHS dentist — sign up via NHS.UK or 111. Unregistered patients use emergency dental services. Dental neglect from access barriers is a major cause of facial sepsis admissions.
Smoking cessation Smoking doubles periodontal disease risk — major cause of dental abscesses and tooth loss. Brief intervention + smoking cessation service referral at every dental/GP consultation.
Salivary gland health Adequate hydration (2 litres/day) reduces salivary calculus formation. Avoid dehydration. Stimulate salivary flow with sugar-free chewing gum. Avoid dry mouth medications where possible.
Allergen avoidance EpiPen — trained use, always carry two pens, not out of date. Medical alert bracelet. Carry action plan. Allergy UK helpline: 01322 619898. Avoid identified trigger.
Sun protection Sjögren's patients have photosensitivity risk. Apply SPF50 daily. Sjögren's also carries 44× increased risk of parotid lymphoma — annual parotid examination.
Eye care (Sjögren's) Preservative-free artificial tears 4–6×/day. Hypromellose 0.3% drops. Avoid dry environments, fans blowing at face. Punctal plugs via ophthalmology if severe.
MMR vaccine check Bilateral parotid swelling in unvaccinated or MMR-unknown → check vaccination status. Two doses of MMR vaccine prevent mumps. Offer catch-up if not vaccinated.
NHS dental access is a significant problem in the UK — approximately 30 million adults are not registered with an NHS dentist (2023 data). This directly contributes to the high rates of dental abscess presentations in emergency departments and general practice. GP prescribing of antibiotics without dental referral perpetuates the problem — always simultaneously refer to emergency dental services. Sjögren's syndrome carries a 44× increased lifetime risk of parotid MALT lymphoma compared to the general population — this is the most important long-term monitoring priority and is managed via annual rheumatology review.

Safety

Follow-Up & Safety-Netting

48 hours
Cellulitis: is erythema spreading despite antibiotics? Mark the leading edge with pen at time of prescribing — compare at 48 hrs. Not improving → hospital admission for IV antibiotics.
1 week
Post-dental abscess treatment — confirm definitive dental treatment arranged (extraction or root canal). Swelling fully resolved? No residual hard mass (if so, exclude tumour).
3 weeks
Any facial lump persisting ≥3 weeks with no clear infective cause → 2WW head and neck referral regardless of soft texture. Do not wait longer.
ACE inhibitor
Confirm switch to ARB documented. Recheck BP at 4 weeks. Warn patient about future facial swelling with any medication. Document allergy status on records.
2WW follow-up
Confirm referral received by hospital. Expected appointment within 2 weeks. If no letter after 2 weeks → chase directly.
999 safety-net
Increasing swelling + difficulty breathing, stridor, drooling, sudden throat tightening, deteriorating consciousness, severe trismus
Same-day GP
Cellulitis not improving at 48 hrs, spreading redness beyond marked margin, new fever/rigors, increasing trismus, new diplopia with periorbital swelling
Marking the leading edge of cellulitis with a surgical skin marker at the time of prescribing antibiotics is a well-validated safety-netting tool — any spread beyond the marked border at 48 hours indicates treatment failure and mandates IV antibiotics in hospital. The 3-week rule for unexplained lumps is based on NICE NG12 — the risk of malignancy in a persistent unexplained lump at 3 weeks is sufficient to justify 2WW referral. Waiting longer for spontaneous resolution risks delayed cancer diagnosis. Facial cellulitis tracking below the mandible into the neck requires same-day hospital admission — downward spread to the mediastinum (descending necrotising mediastinitis) carries 40% mortality even with surgical intervention.
Educational use only. Based on NICE CKS Dental Abscess (2023), NICE NG12 (Suspected Cancer Referral), NICE CKS Salivary Gland Conditions, BSACI Angio-oedema guidelines, NICE CG37 (Referral for Suspected Cancer), BDA guidelines. Always adapt to individual patient context.
