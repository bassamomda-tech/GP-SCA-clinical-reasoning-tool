📋 Clinical Stem — Acute Neurological Deficit Presenting to Primary Care
A patient presents to a GP surgery with sudden-onset neurological symptoms — is this a TIA, a stroke, or a mimic?
Mrs Beatrice Okafor, 68, a retired teaching assistant, presents as a same-day urgent appointment. Her daughter called reception 45 minutes ago saying "Mum's left hand went numb and she couldn't speak properly for about 20 minutes — it's come back now but I'm worried." Mrs Okafor is known to the practice with atrial fibrillation (not currently anticoagulated — declined warfarin 18 months ago), hypertension, and type 2 diabetes. Her BP at her last review was 158/94. She appears well now but her daughter is in the room and visibly anxious.
This stem tests whether the candidate recognises a resolved TIA as a neurological emergency despite the patient "feeling fine now," addresses the anticoagulation gap in AF, manages a worried relative, and demonstrates urgency without panic. The stem can be adapted for different demographics, risk profiles, and symptom durations to practise different ABCD² scores and referral pathways.
Scenario A — Resolved TIA (high ABCD²) Symptoms fully resolved within 60 minutes but ABCD² ≥4 (AF, DM, age >60). Requires same-day specialist assessment within 24 hours. Tests urgency recognition despite apparently well patient.
Scenario B — Ongoing stroke symptoms Patient still has facial droop and arm weakness after 90 minutes. FAST-positive. Tests whether candidate activates 999 immediately rather than continuing to examine or investigate in primary care.
Scenario C — Low-risk TIA (ABCD² <4) Single episode of isolated sensory symptoms lasting 5 minutes in a 42-year-old with no vascular risk factors. Tests whether candidate still arranges specialist review within 7 days and starts aspirin 300mg.
Scenario D — Stroke mimic (migraine aura) 35-year-old woman with visual scintillations followed by unilateral numbness, then headache. Known migraineur. Tests ability to differentiate mimic from TIA while maintaining safety-netting.
Scenario E — Young stroke (dissection) 38-year-old man with sudden neck pain after gym session, followed by Horner syndrome and contralateral weakness. Tests recognition of cervical artery dissection as a stroke cause in young adults.
Key variables to adapt for Age (young stroke vs elderly); AF status (anticoagulated vs not); symptom duration (resolved vs ongoing); ABCD² score components; ethnicity (Afro-Caribbean higher stroke risk); diabetes; prior TIA history; patient's attitude to anticoagulation; carer/relative in the room.

Step 1
History Taking — Open Question First · Targeted Questions · ICE · Psychosocial Context
▲collapse
In TIA and stroke, history is the single most important diagnostic tool — imaging confirms, but history diagnoses. A TIA is a clinical diagnosis made entirely from the history: sudden-onset focal neurological deficit of presumed vascular origin that resolves within 24 hours (usually <60 minutes). The GP's job is to establish the time course, characterise the deficit, risk-stratify with ABCD², identify the underlying mechanism (AF, carotid, small vessel), and simultaneously address the patient's fear and the relative's anxiety — all within a time-pressured consultation.
🎓 Consultation opener — use existing information first
"Good morning Mrs Okafor. I can see from the notes that your daughter called because you had some sudden numbness and difficulty speaking earlier — that must have been quite frightening. I'd really like to hear what happened in your own words."
This acknowledges the information already available (daughter's call, known AF and HTN) without re-asking. Asking "So what brings you in today?" when the reason is already documented loses marks in Relating to Others for failing to use available information, and in Global Skills for wasting time.

Safeguarding Considerations — Consider in Every Consultation

Stroke and TIA can be both a consequence and a marker of harm. Traumatic brain injury from domestic violence can cause stroke in young adults. Post-stroke cognitive impairment creates new vulnerability to financial abuse, neglect, and coercive control. A patient who "keeps falling" may be experiencing physical abuse. Always consider the social context behind the neurological event.
🏠 Domestic Abuse / Intimate Partner Violence
  • Traumatic carotid/vertebral dissection from strangulation or blows to the head/neck — a leading cause of young stroke
  • Unexplained bruising on the neck, face, or scalp in a patient presenting with TIA symptoms
  • Partner who is excessively controlling during the consultation, answers all questions, or refuses to leave the room
  • Repeated presentations with "falls" or head injuries preceding neurological symptoms
👴 Older Adults / Carer-related Concern
  • Post-stroke patients are highly vulnerable to neglect — inability to self-care, communicate needs, or mobilise independently
  • Missed medications (antihypertensives, anticoagulants) may indicate carer neglect rather than patient non-adherence
  • Financial abuse: post-stroke cognitive impairment may be exploited by family members or carers seeking control of finances
  • Unexplained pressure sores, malnutrition, or dehydration in a stroke survivor at home
🧒 Children in the Household
  • A parent having a stroke creates sudden caring responsibilities for children — assess for young carers who may miss school
  • If the patient with TIA/stroke is the primary carer for dependent children, ensure immediate childcare safety
  • Children witnessing a parent's stroke can experience significant psychological trauma — signpost support
  • If domestic violence is suspected as the cause, consider safeguarding referral for all children in the household
💊 Self-Harm / Medication Misuse Risk
  • Post-stroke depression affects 33% of survivors — actively screen for suicidal ideation at every review
  • Intentional non-adherence to anticoagulation ("I'd rather die than take another tablet") may be a cry for help
  • Overdose of anticoagulants as a method of self-harm — ask specifically about this in at-risk patients
  • Social isolation after stroke (inability to drive, loss of independence) is a major risk factor for depression and self-harm
If a safeguarding concern is identified: Document verbatim what was said and observed. If the patient is in immediate danger, contact the police. For non-immediate concerns, discuss with the practice safeguarding lead within 24 hours. In all cases, record the concern using the practice safeguarding template, flag the notes, and arrange a follow-up appointment within 1 week. For post-stroke vulnerability, consider a social services referral and occupational therapy assessment of the home environment.

1D — ICE: Ideas · Concerns · Expectations — in every consultation, not just SCA
💡 Why ICE matters in TIA & Stroke — not a tick-box exercise

A TIA is terrifying — the patient experienced sudden loss of brain function and then recovered. Many patients oscillate between "it was nothing, I feel fine now" (denial minimising the event) and "am I going to have a full stroke?" (catastrophic fear). The patient's idea of what happened determines whether they accept urgent referral and medication changes. If Mrs Okafor still believes warfarin is "rat poison" (her reason for declining anticoagulation 18 months ago), starting a DOAC today requires addressing that belief directly. ICE here isn't just communication — it's the gateway to life-saving secondary prevention.

💭 Ideas
"What do you think caused what happened this morning? Sometimes people have a theory — I'd really like to hear yours."
Many patients attribute TIA symptoms to stress, tiredness, or "a funny turn." If the patient doesn't connect the event to cerebrovascular disease, they won't understand why urgent action is needed. Correcting the internal model is essential before discussing anticoagulation or aspirin loading.
😟 Concerns
"What's been worrying you most since this happened? Sometimes the worry is about the event itself, and sometimes it's about something else entirely."
The hidden concern is rarely "will I have a stroke?" — it's more often "will I lose my independence?", "will I be able to look after my grandchildren?", or "my father died of a stroke at 70." The daughter's anxiety is also a concern to address. Naming the specific fear allows targeted reassurance and builds trust for the management plan.
🎯 Expectations
"What were you hoping we could do for you today? I want to make sure we're working towards the same goal."
Some patients expect a brain scan today (GP can't arrange emergency MRI). Some expect to be told everything is fine. Some expect a referral letter they can take to A&E themselves. Understanding the expectation allows you to meet it where possible, explain when you can't, and always offer something concrete. In this case, the patient may not expect to be told she needs hospital within 24 hours — managing that surprise is a key communication skill.
1E — Psychosocial context: the person behind the neurological event
🫂 Social determinants drive cerebrovascular risk — stroke is a disease of inequality

Stroke incidence is 2× higher in the most deprived quintile compared to the least deprived. Poverty drives exposure to every modifiable stroke risk factor: hypertension (stress, poor diet, limited healthcare access), diabetes (food deserts, sedentary environments), smoking (higher prevalence in deprived areas), AF (under-detected in communities with poor primary care access), and medication non-adherence (cost, health literacy, language barriers). Understanding the patient's social context isn't optional — it determines which interventions are realistic and which will fail.

🏠 Housing & Living Situation

A patient who lives alone after a TIA is at higher risk of unwitnessed stroke. Stairs, bathroom access, and kitchen safety become critical if residual deficit develops. Post-stroke discharge planning starts at the first consultation.

"Who's at home with you? If something like this happened again when you were on your own, would someone know?"

If alone: arrange lifeline/personal alarm, involve next of kin in safety-netting, and lower threshold for hospital admission.

💷 Financial Stress & Deprivation

Prescription costs may deter medication adherence — many stroke prevention drugs are lifelong. Inability to drive post-TIA affects employment. Loss of income compounds stress, raising BP further in a vicious cycle.

"I want to make sure cost doesn't get in the way of your treatment. Are you aware of the NHS prescription prepayment certificate?"

Over-60s get free prescriptions. For under-60s, signpost PPC (£30.25/3 months). Refer to Citizens Advice for benefits if employment affected.

🗣️ Health Literacy & Language

Understanding of TIA varies enormously: "mini-stroke" may be dismissed as trivial, or may be conflated with a full stroke causing panic. Patients with limited English may not understand safety-netting instructions — a life-threatening gap.

"I want to make sure I've explained this clearly. In your own words, what would make you call 999?"

Use teach-back method. Provide written safety-net in patient's first language. Book professional interpreter for follow-up if needed.

👨‍👩‍👧 Caring Responsibilities

This patient's daughter brought her in — but who does Mrs Okafor care for? Many 68-year-olds are primary carers for grandchildren or a spouse. A TIA that becomes a stroke could leave dependants suddenly without care.

"Are there people who depend on you day-to-day — grandchildren, your husband, anyone you help regularly?"

If caring responsibilities identified: involve social services proactively, plan emergency care arrangements, and document them in the notes.

😰 Anxiety & Psychological Impact

Post-TIA anxiety is under-recognised: 30% of patients develop clinically significant anxiety within 3 months. Fear of recurrence may lead to avoidance behaviours (refusing to leave the house, refusing to be alone). The daughter's visible anxiety in the room compounds the patient's own distress.

"It's completely normal to feel frightened after what happened. How are you feeling about things emotionally — and how about you, [daughter's name]?"

Normalise anxiety. Offer psychology referral via IAPT. Include the relative explicitly — their anxiety affects the patient's recovery.

🚗 Transport & Access to Care

DVLA rules prohibit driving for 1 month after TIA. If the patient relies on driving for shopping, appointments, or work, this restriction has immediate practical impact. Rural patients without public transport may become housebound.

"I need to talk to you about driving — I know that might feel like a lot on top of everything else. How do you normally get around?"

Document DVLA advice. Offer hospital transport for specialist appointment. Signpost community transport services and voluntary driver schemes.

Triage Engine — Emergency · Urgent · Routine
▲collapse
TIA and stroke triage is binary at first contact: are symptoms still present? If yes → 999 (stroke). If resolved → ABCD² score determines urgency of specialist referral. The GP's role is not to diagnose the type of stroke — it is to recognise the syndrome, activate the correct pathway, and start immediate secondary prevention (aspirin 300mg). Every decision in this step is time-critical: stroke is a "brain attack" with the same urgency as a heart attack.
🔴 Emergency

Step 5
Reaching a Diagnosis & DDx — Explained in Plain Language
▲collapse
TIA is a clinical diagnosis — there is no single test that "proves" it. The GP must share a working diagnosis in plain language, explain why the event happened, address the patient's own explanation ("it was just stress"), and name the differential diagnoses the specialist will rule out. The diagnosis must be shared in lay terms that motivate the patient to accept urgent treatment — not medical jargon that creates confusion or false reassurance.
🗣️ Explaining the Diagnosis in Plain Language — say something like this

"What happened to you this morning was something called a TIA — sometimes people call it a 'mini-stroke.' I know that sounds scary, so let me explain. Your brain needs a constant blood supply, like water through a pipe. What happened is that a small clot briefly blocked one of those pipes, which is why your hand went numb and your speech was affected. The clot cleared on its own, which is why you feel normal now. But here's why I'm taking this seriously: a TIA is a warning sign. It tells us that the plumbing in your brain is vulnerable, and if we don't act now — with medication and lifestyle changes — there's a real risk of a full stroke, where the blockage doesn't clear. The good news is that most strokes after TIA are preventable if we start treatment today."

💬 Addressing the patient's own explanation — why it may not be the full picture

"I thought it was just stress — I've been under a lot of pressure lately."
"Stress is real and it absolutely affects your body — it can raise blood pressure and make your heart more vulnerable. But what you described — the sudden numbness and speech difficulty that came on like flicking a switch — that pattern is very specific. Stress alone wouldn't cause that. What it tells me is that something happened to the blood supply to your brain, even though you feel fine now."

"It only lasted 20 minutes — surely if it was serious it would have lasted longer?"
"Actually, it's the opposite. The fact that it was brief and got better tells me it was a TIA — the clot cleared on its own. A longer episode that didn't get better would be a full stroke. But a TIA is your brain's way of sending an early warning, and we have a window right now to prevent something bigger. That's why I don't want to wait."

A — Diagnosable in Primary Care
GP can diagnose
TIA (transient ischaemic attack) — sudden-onset focal neurological deficit of presumed vascular origin, fully resolved within 24 hours (usually <60 minutes). Diagnosed clinically from history + risk factor profile + absence of residual deficit. ABCD² score stratifies urgency.
Hypertensive emergency with neurological symptoms — severely elevated BP (>180/120) causing headache, visual disturbance, or confusion. Symptoms resolve with BP lowering. Requires same-day management but different pathway to TIA.
Migraine with aura (TIA mimic) — visual scintillations, "marching" paraesthesia over 20–30 minutes, followed by headache. Positive phenomena (flashing lights, tingling) rather than negative (weakness, numbness). Known migraineur. GP can diagnose confidently if classic features present.
B — Suspected — Refer
Refer for confirmation

Ischaemic stroke (minor)

Symptoms lasted >24 hours or MRI DWI-positive despite symptom resolution. May present as "TIA" to GP but specialist imaging reclassifies as stroke. Same acute management pathway.

Carotid artery stenosis (symptomatic)

Anterior circulation TIA/amaurosis fugax with ipsilateral carotid bruit. Requires carotid Doppler to grade stenosis. 50–99% = endarterectomy within 2 weeks.

Paroxysmal AF (undiagnosed)

TIA with no obvious mechanism in a patient in sinus rhythm. Holter monitoring or implantable loop recorder may detect paroxysmal AF as the embolic source. Changes treatment from antiplatelet to anticoagulation.

Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis

Headache + focal deficit + seizure, especially in young women on COCP. CT venogram confirms. Anticoagulation is the treatment (even if haemorrhagic transformation present).

C — Emergency — Act Now
Diagnose & act

Acute ischaemic stroke (major)

Ongoing FAST-positive deficit. Thrombolysis (alteplase) window is 4.5 hours from symptom onset. Thrombectomy window up to 24 hours for large vessel occlusion. Every minute delay = 1.9 million neurons lost. 999 with stroke pre-alert.

Intracerebral / subarachnoid haemorrhage

Thunderclap headache + focal deficit + reduced GCS. CT head within 1 hour. Neurosurgical emergency. No antiplatelet, no anticoagulation until haemorrhage excluded by CT.

Cervical artery dissection

Young adult with neck pain + Horner syndrome + contralateral hemiparesis. May follow trauma or chiropractic manipulation. CT angiography diagnostic. Anticoagulation for 3–6 months.

Mediterranean-style diet. Reduce salt to < 6 g/day. Increase fruit/veg to 5+ portions. Reduce saturated fat. DASH diet if hypertension is primary driver.
🏃 Exercise
150 min/week moderate aerobic (brisk walking, swimming). Start gradually after specialist clearance. Reduces BP by 5-8 mmHg. Improves insulin sensitivity.
⚖️ Weight
Target BMI 18.5-24.9 (adjust for ethnicity — Mrs Okafor: BMI target < 23 for South Asian / Black African origin). Waist circumference < 80 cm (women). Refer to weight management if BMI > 30.
🧠 Mental Health
Screen for depression / anxiety (PHQ-9, GAD-7) at 6-week follow-up. Post-stroke depression affects ~33 % of patients. Early intervention improves recovery and medication adherence.
