📋 Clinical Stem — Hypothyroidism
A patient presents with fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, constipation, dry skin, or hair loss — or is found to have an elevated TSH on routine or targeted blood testing.
"Ms Sarah Brennan is a 42-year-old teacher who presents with a 4-month history of increasing fatigue, weight gain of 5 kg, feeling cold all the time, worsening constipation, dry skin, and thinning hair. She has been told she is 'probably depressed' and was recently started on sertraline by a locum GP without thyroid function tests being checked. She takes calcium carbonate 500 mg daily for osteoporosis prevention. She is not pregnant but is trying to conceive."
Hypothyroidism is one of the most commonly missed diagnoses in primary care. Symptoms are insidious, non-specific, and frequently attributed to depression, age, or lifestyle. The framework addresses: confirming the diagnosis (TSH + free T4), identifying the cause (Hashimoto's — TPO antibodies), safe initiation of levothyroxine, critical drug interactions, and special populations (pregnancy, cardiac, elderly).
Scenario A — Overt hypothyroidism TSH >10 + low free T4 + symptoms. Start levothyroxine 50 mcg OD. Recheck TSH + free T4 in 6–8 weeks. Titrate in 25 mcg increments.
Scenario B — Subclinical hypothyroidism TSH 4–10 + normal free T4 ± symptoms. If TSH >10: treat. If TSH 4–10: individualise (symptoms, age, TPO antibodies, pregnancy plans). Two elevated TSH results required before treatment.
Scenario C — Hypothyroidism in pregnancy TSH >2.5 in first trimester. Urgent obstetric referral. Start levothyroxine immediately. TSH target <2.5 throughout first trimester. Increase dose by 25–30% immediately when pregnancy confirmed.
Scenario D — Elderly / cardiac patient Start at 25 mcg OD (not 50 mcg). Titrate slowly (25 mcg every 4–6 weeks). Risk of precipitating angina or arrhythmia with too-rapid increase in cardiac patients.
Scenario E — Hashimoto's thyroiditis Elevated TPO antibodies + elevated TSH. Autoimmune cause. Associated with other autoimmune conditions (type 1 diabetes, coeliac, vitiligo, Addison's). Family history relevant. Higher risk of progression from subclinical to overt hypothyroidism.
Scenario F — Myxoedema coma Severe hypothyroidism + hypothermia + confusion + bradycardia + hypoventilation. Life-threatening emergency — 999 immediately. IV T4 (levothyroxine). ITU admission. Rare but must not be missed.

Step 1
History Taking — Open Question First · Targeted Questions · ICE · Psychosocial Context
▲collapse
Hypothyroidism history has two layers: the symptom picture (non-specific and easily attributed elsewhere) and the clinical context (risk factors, family history, drug causes, special populations). The classic symptom cluster — fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, constipation, dry skin, hair loss, bradycardia, slowed reflexes — often develops insidiously over months to years. Many patients have been labelled with depression, menopause, or IBS before thyroid disease is diagnosed. ICE in hypothyroidism must address the patient's existing diagnosis (often depression) and their understanding of what the thyroid does.
🎓 Consultation opener
"I can see from your notes that you've been feeling very tired recently and have been started on an antidepressant. Before we talk about that, I'd like to ask a few questions and check your thyroid — a small gland in your neck that controls your metabolism. Can you tell me more about how you've been feeling?"
Reference the existing information from the stem (fatigue, sertraline started) before asking open questions. This demonstrates preparation, saves time, and immediately builds rapport. In SCA, it gains a mark in the Global Skills domain before you have asked a single clinical question.

1C — ICE: Ideas · Concerns · Expectations
💡 Why ICE matters in Hypothyroidism

Patients presenting with hypothyroidism have almost always been told something else is causing their symptoms — depression, menopause, stress, age, or "just the way you are." Many have been treated for depression without response. The diagnosis of hypothyroidism often brings profound relief ("finally someone found the cause") but also concerns about lifelong medication, weight that might not return to normal, and whether the antidepressant they were started on was appropriate. ICE in hypothyroidism addresses all of these — and creates the therapeutic alliance that ensures levothyroxine is taken correctly.

💭 Ideas
"What was your own understanding of what was causing all this — the tiredness, weight gain, and feeling cold? Had anyone suggested what might be behind it?"
Many patients will say "I was told it was depression" or "I thought it was menopause." Others will have Googled their symptoms and already suspect thyroid disease. Establishing their existing model determines whether you are confirming a diagnosis they expect or reframing one they have already been given.
😟 Concerns
"Is there something you've been particularly worried about — whether about the diagnosis itself, or about taking medication long-term?"
Common concerns: Is this serious? Will I gain even more weight? Do I have to take tablets forever? Will the antidepressant I was started on make things worse? Will this affect my fertility? In SCA, identifying and addressing these concerns earns multiple Relating to Others domain marks.
🎯 Expectations
"What were you hoping would happen today — were you expecting a particular treatment, or hoping for an explanation?"
Patients with hypothyroidism often expect and want immediate symptom resolution. Managing this expectation is clinically important — symptoms improve gradually over 3–6 months on levothyroxine, not overnight. Setting this expectation prevents premature dose escalation based on ongoing symptoms rather than TSH level.

Step 4
Do I Need This Investigation?
▲collapse
TSH is the first-line investigation — alone it both diagnoses and guides treatment in most cases. Elevated TSH + low free T4 = overt hypothyroidism. Elevated TSH + normal free T4 = subclinical hypothyroidism. TPO antibodies identify Hashimoto's (autoimmune cause) and predict progression from subclinical to overt hypothyroidism.

TSH (thyroid-stimulating hormone)First-line and most sensitive test for thyroid dysfunction. A normal TSH virtually excludes hypothyroidism. Elevated TSH indicates the pituitary is "calling for" more thyroid hormone — the defining test for primary hypothyroidism. Must be confirmed on a second sample before treatment in subclinical cases (TSH <10).TSH >10: treat regardless of symptoms. TSH 4–10: individualise. TSH normal: hypothyroidism excluded as cause of symptoms — investigate other causes.

Step 5
Reaching a Diagnosis & DDx — Explained in Plain Language
▲collapse
Explaining hypothyroidism in plain language requires making the thyroid's role in metabolism concrete and understandable. The "thermostat" analogy is the most effective: "Your thyroid is like the thermostat that controls your metabolism — it sets the speed at which your body works. When it's underactive, everything slows down: your metabolism, your bowels, your thinking, your heart rate." This one analogy explains fatigue, weight gain, constipation, cold intolerance, and cognitive slowing simultaneously.
🗣️ Diagnosis in Plain Language

"Your blood tests confirm that your thyroid gland is underactive. The thyroid is like the body's thermostat — it controls how fast your metabolism runs. When it's underactive, everything slows down: your energy, your digestion, your ability to keep warm, your mood, and even your thinking. This explains every symptom you've been having — the fatigue, weight gain, constipation, feeling cold, hair thinning, and the low mood. Importantly, this is not depression — or at least not the primary cause. I think the antidepressant you were started on recently may not have been the right first step, because the root cause is the thyroid. The good news is that this is very treatable with a simple tablet called levothyroxine — it replaces the hormone your thyroid should be making. Once your thyroid levels are right, most of your symptoms should improve significantly over the next 3 to 6 months."

💬 Addressing common patient concerns

"Will I have to take this tablet forever?"
"Most people with autoimmune hypothyroidism — which is what this appears to be — do need to take it long-term. The good news is that it's a simple once-daily tablet with very few side effects when the dose is right, and many people feel dramatically better once they're on the right dose. We'll monitor your blood tests regularly to make sure we keep it optimised."

"Will the weight come off once I start the tablet?"
"It often does improve — you may lose 2–3 kg once your metabolism returns to normal. However, I want to be honest: the thyroid replacement usually helps with the metabolic component, but if other factors like diet and activity are contributing, those will still need attention. I don't want to promise a transformation that isn't certain — but the metabolic contribution from the thyroid should improve."

"What about the antidepressant — should I stop it?"
"I wouldn't stop it abruptly without a plan — that needs careful thought. Once your thyroid levels are treated and normalised, we can review your mood again and decide together whether the sertraline is still needed. Many people find that their mood improves significantly with the thyroid treatment alone. Let's give it 3 months and see."

A — Primary care diagnosis and management

Hashimoto's thyroiditis (autoimmune)

Most common cause (70–80%). TPO antibodies positive. May fluctuate. Associated with other autoimmune conditions. Lifelong levothyroxine in most cases.

Post-thyroidectomy or post-radioiodine

Iatrogenic — lifelong replacement mandatory. Dose requirements may change with age. TSH target depends on indication (suppressed if thyroid cancer, normal range for benign).

Iodine deficiency (rare in UK)

Endemic hypothyroidism from dietary iodine deficiency. More common in pregnancy and in populations avoiding iodised salt and dairy. Resolves with adequate iodine.

B — Drug-induced / Specialist input

Amiodarone-induced hypothyroidism

TSH elevation in patient on amiodarone (37% iodine). Do NOT stop amiodarone without cardiology — can precipitate life-threatening arrhythmia. Levothyroxine prescribed alongside.

Lithium-induced hypothyroidism

Up to 40% of long-term lithium users develop hypothyroidism. Coordinate with psychiatry before modifying lithium. Levothyroxine prescribed alongside ongoing lithium in most cases.

Subclinical hypothyroidism (TSH 4–10)

Individualise treatment — consider symptoms, TPO antibodies, age, pregnancy plans. Two elevated TSH results required before treatment. Annual TSH if not treating.

C — Emergency / Urgent

Myxoedema coma

Severe hypothyroidism + hypothermia + confusion + bradycardia. 999. IV levothyroxine. ITU. Mortality 25–60%. Precipitants: cold exposure, infection, opioids.

Hypothyroidism in pregnancy

Any elevation in TSH in pregnancy. Urgent obstetric referral. Levothyroxine immediately. TSH target <2.5 in first trimester. Foetal neurodevelopment at risk.

Levothyroxine is one of the most prescribed drugs in UK primary care — yet it is also one of the most commonly suboptimally managed. The key principles are: start low in elderly/cardiac patients, titrate in 25 mcg increments every 6–8 weeks, take on an empty stomach 30 minutes before food, separate from calcium/iron/PPIs by 4 hours, target TSH in the lower half of normal range for symptom relief, and never titrate by symptoms alone — always recheck TSH before changing dose. In SCA, demonstrating knowledge of the interaction profile and the 6–8 week rechecking window earns specific Tasks domain marks.
7A — Address the patient's expectation first
🤝
Validate → explain → negotiate — in that order
1
Validate — name their expectation

Most patients expect to start a tablet and feel better quickly. Many are also frustrated that the antidepressant was started without checking the thyroid. Both of these expectations deserve direct acknowledgement before the clinical plan.

"You've been struggling for months and I completely understand the frustration — particularly that this wasn't picked up earlier. I want to put that right today."
2
Explain — share your clinical reasoning

The response to levothyroxine is gradual — symptoms improve over 3–6 months, not days. Setting this expectation prevents premature dose escalation based on ongoing symptoms. The thermostat analogy explains why it takes time: the body needs to readjust all its systems to a higher metabolic rate.

"The tablet works by gradually turning up the thermostat — your body has been running slow for months and it takes time for everything to recalibrate. Most people start feeling better within 4–6 weeks, but the full benefit takes 3–6 months."
3
Negotiate — offer something concrete today

Give the prescription today with specific counselling on timing. Book the 6–8 week blood test appointment before the patient leaves. Address the calcium interaction immediately — this is actionable today and prevents treatment failure.

"I'm going to prescribe this today — and the most important thing about taking it correctly is timing: take it on an empty stomach, 30 minutes before breakfast, and your calcium tablet at a completely different time — lunchtime or evening. I'm booking your blood test for 6 weeks."
Key principle: Correct levothyroxine timing counselling is the single most important practical instruction you can give. The patient who takes levothyroxine at the same time as their calcium supplement will have significantly reduced absorption and will feel no better — and may be labelled as "treatment-refractory" when in fact they are simply taking it incorrectly.

7C — Non-medication management
Lifestyle measures support but do not replace levothyroxine in overt hypothyroidism. However, they are clinically relevant: dietary selenium supports thyroid peroxidase enzyme function; iodine sufficiency is essential; physical activity reduces the metabolic consequences of hypothyroidism during the titration period; stress management reduces HPA axis cortisol, which blunts T4 to T3 conversion.
⏰
Levothyroxine Timing
Take fasting, 30 min before breakfast; consistent time daily
Mechanism

Levothyroxine absorption is significantly reduced by food (particularly high-fibre and calcium-rich foods), coffee, and certain medications. An empty stomach maximises absorption. Consistency of timing improves TSH stability and reduces dose variation.

Practical

Take levothyroxine first thing in the morning — before getting up if possible — with a full glass of water. Wait 30 minutes before breakfast. Set a phone alarm if helpful. Bedtime dosing is an evidence-based alternative for those who cannot take it fasting in the morning.

Consistent fasting administration improves levothyroxine bioavailability by up to 40% vs with food
⚠️
Drug Interaction Management
Separate calcium, iron, PPIs by 4 hours
Mechanism

Calcium carbonate, calcium citrate, ferrous sulphate/fumarate, and PPIs (especially omeprazole) all reduce levothyroxine absorption by 20–40%. Cholestyramine, sucralfate, and antacids also interfere. This is the most common cause of apparent treatment failure in primary care.

Practical

This patient takes calcium carbonate daily. Specific instruction: levothyroxine at 7am before breakfast; calcium carbonate with lunch or at bedtime — minimum 4 hours apart. Write this down for the patient and document it in the notes as a counselling point.

Correct separation from calcium/iron/PPIs can restore TSH to target without needing a dose increase
🥗
Iodine Sufficiency
Adequate dietary iodine; avoid excessive seaweed or supplements
Mechanism

Iodine is required for thyroid hormone synthesis. In the UK, iodine deficiency is rare but emerging (particularly in vegans who avoid dairy). Excessive iodine supplementation (seaweed, high-dose iodine supplements) can paradoxically worsen Hashimoto's by triggering immune activation (Wolff-Chaikoff effect).

Practical

Normal UK diet with dairy provides adequate iodine. Vegetarians/vegans: consider iodine-fortified foods or low-dose supplement (150 mcg/day — not kelp-based which has variable high iodine content). Avoid high-dose iodine supplements entirely in Hashimoto's.

Adequate dietary iodine maintains optimal T4 synthesis alongside levothyroxine replacement
🏃
Physical Activity
Gentle, consistent activity during titration period
Mechanism

Hypothyroidism reduces cardiac output and muscle metabolism, making vigorous exercise difficult. During the titration period, gentle consistent activity (walking, swimming) maintains cardiovascular fitness and mental wellbeing without overloading an underperforming cardiac system.

Practical

Start with 20–30 minute daily walks; build up as energy improves. Avoid pushing through severe fatigue — this is physiological, not lack of effort. As TSH normalises, energy and exercise tolerance typically improve significantly.

Consistent gentle activity during treatment improves mood, maintains muscle mass, and supports metabolic recovery
🧠
Mental Wellbeing During Treatment
Manage expectations; recognise improving vs persistent depression
Mechanism

Hypothyroid-driven depression typically improves with thyroid treatment alone. However, it may take 3–6 months to resolve fully. If mood is not improving at 3 months despite TSH in target range, primary depression may be coexisting and antidepressant review is warranted.

Practical

PHQ-9 at each review. Do not abruptly stop the sertraline — review at 3-month thyroid check. If TSH is now normal and mood is not improving: maintain antidepressant. If TSH is normal and mood is significantly better: discuss tapering plan. Never stop sertraline abruptly.

Mood typically improves alongside TSH normalisation; sertraline review at 3 months is clinically appropriate
🤰
Preconception and Pregnancy Planning
TSH <2.5 before conception; increase dose by 25–30% on positive test
Mechanism

Foetal thyroid function is not established until 16–20 weeks of gestation. Before this, the foetus is entirely dependent on maternal T4 for neurodevelopment. Even mild maternal hypothyroidism in the first trimester causes irreversible foetal neurological impairment.

Practical

This patient is trying to conceive: optimise TSH to <2.5 before conception. When pregnancy test is positive: increase levothyroxine dose by 25–30% immediately (add 2 extra doses per week is a practical rule), recheck TSH in 4 weeks. Refer to obstetric endocrinology at booking appointment.

TSH <2.5 in first trimester prevents foetal neurodevelopmental impairment — the most important treatment target in this case
7D — Levothyroxine dosing guide
Levothyroxine dose is based on body weight (~1.6 mcg/kg/day ideal body weight) but is always initiated at a low starting dose and titrated. Never start at the full calculated dose in one step — this risks over-replacement symptoms (palpitations, anxiety, insomnia) and is dangerous in cardiac patients. Titrate in 25 mcg increments every 6–8 weeks, guided by TSH — never by symptoms alone.
Standard initiation (otherwise well adult <65)

Levothyroxine 50 mcg OD — fasting, 30 min before breakfast

  • Start 50 mcg OD; recheck TSH + free T4 at 6–8 weeks
  • If TSH still elevated: increase by 25 mcg; recheck at 6–8 weeks
  • Continue in 25 mcg steps until TSH in target range (0.5–2.5 mU/L)
  • Most adults require 75–150 mcg/day at maintenance
  • Target: TSH in lower half of normal range for symptom relief
Never increase dose based on ongoing symptoms without rechecking TSH first — symptoms lag 6–8 weeks behind TSH normalisation.
Elderly or cardiac patient (>65 or significant IHD/arrhythmia)

Levothyroxine 25 mcg OD — very slow titration

  • Start 25 mcg OD; increase by 25 mcg every 4–6 weeks
  • ECG before initiation in significant cardiac disease
  • Too-rapid replacement precipitates angina, arrhythmia, or cardiac failure
  • Accept a higher TSH (3–6 mU/L) if cardiac stability requires it
  • Cardiology input if significant IHD, LV impairment, or arrhythmia history
Slow titration is not undertreating — it is safe prescribing. Under-replacement symptoms must be balanced against cardiac risk of over-rapid treatment.
Pregnancy and trying to conceive

Levothyroxine — start immediately; urgent titration

  • TSH <2.5 mU/L in 1st trimester — more urgent target than general population
  • Start 50–75 mcg OD; recheck TSH in 4 weeks (not 6–8 weeks — more urgent)
  • When pregnancy confirmed: increase dose by 25–30% immediately (add 2 extra doses/week)
  • TSH targets: 1st trimester <2.5; 2nd/3rd trimester <3.0
  • Post-partum: reduce back to pre-pregnancy dose; recheck TSH at 6 weeks post-partum
Do not wait for specialist appointment before starting — foetal neurodevelopment risk is immediate. Refer to obstetric endocrinology simultaneously, not instead.
When TSH does not respond to levothyroxine — causes of apparent treatment failure
  • Poor adherence: Most common cause — missed doses; irregular timing; never changed behaviour from "as needed" mental model
  • Drug interaction (calcium, iron, PPI, cholestyramine): Reduces absorption by 20–40%; correct timing before escalating dose
  • Malabsorption: Coeliac disease (check anti-tTG), inflammatory bowel disease, or bowel resection — switch to liquid levothyroxine or soft-gel capsule formulation
  • Dose too low for body weight: Recalculate target dose (~1.6 mcg/kg ideal body weight)
  • Other causes of elevated TSH: Non-compliance, recent acute illness (TSH transiently rises), assay interference (biotin supplementation falsely lowers TSH in some assays)
Persistent symptoms despite TSH in target — what to do
  • Review TSH target: Aim for lower half of normal range (0.5–2.0 mU/L) — some patients feel better with a lower-normal TSH
  • Check free T4: Should be in mid-normal to upper-normal range; if low-normal TSH with low-normal T4, patient may be relatively under-replaced
  • Exclude other causes: Anaemia, coeliac disease, depression, fibromyalgia, sleep apnoea — all produce similar symptoms
  • T3/T4 combination: Controversial; not routinely recommended in NICE or BTA guidelines; specialist decision only; has cardiac risk (T3 has shorter half-life, harder to titrate safely)
  • Endocrinology referral: If TSH consistently in range and symptoms persist after thorough primary care review

7G — Psychosocial impact of the diagnosis
🫂
Hypothyroidism affects identity, mood, relationships, and professional life — often before the diagnosis is made
The psychological impact of hypothyroidism extends beyond the biochemical. Patients with undiagnosed hypothyroidism often internalise their symptoms as personal failures — "I'm lazy," "I can't cope," "I'm gaining weight because of my diet." The diagnosis can bring relief but also grief (for the years of misattributed symptoms), frustration (at the delayed diagnosis), and anxiety about lifelong medication. The GP's role is to reframe the narrative and support the transition from "unwell without explanation" to "managed chronic condition."
💼
Work and Cognitive Function

Cognitive slowing, poor concentration, and memory difficulties from hypothyroidism significantly impair work performance — particularly in cognitively demanding roles. Teachers, managers, and professionals often experience a progressive decline in performance they attribute to "burning out."

A fit note is a legitimate and compassionate option for patients with severe symptomatic hypothyroidism during the titration period — particularly if the patient is a teacher managing 30 children while profoundly hypothyroid.

Framing the cognitive improvement as expected — "your thinking will improve as the levels come right" — provides hope and a concrete endpoint.

"A lot of the difficulty you've been having at work — the tiredness, difficulty concentrating — these are very likely to improve significantly as your thyroid levels come up to normal. It's the thyroid, not you."
⚖️
Weight and Body Image

Weight gain from hypothyroidism is frequently catastrophised by patients as "I've lost control" — and just as frequently dismissed by clinicians as "lifestyle." Reframing weight gain as a direct metabolic consequence of thyroid failure is one of the most important and restorative things a GP can do in this consultation.

However, honest expectation management is equally important: levothyroxine typically reduces hypothyroid-related weight by 2–5 kg. It does not produce dramatic weight loss if other factors are contributing.

Patients who expect complete weight normalisation and do not achieve it may lose faith in the treatment — and in themselves. Set a realistic, honest expectation.

"The weight gain you've experienced is a direct consequence of your thyroid slowing your metabolism down — it is not your fault. Most people lose 2–3 kg once the thyroid is treated, but I want to be honest that it's not a complete fix for weight."
😔
Depression and Misdiagnosis

In this case, the patient was started on an antidepressant without TFTs being checked — a common and consequential error in primary care. Addressing this sensitively without undermining the previous GP or causing the patient to lose confidence in primary care as a whole is a clinically challenging communication task.

Frame it as "a new finding that changes the picture" rather than "a mistake that was made." This is not dishonest — it is exactly what has happened: new information has become available.

The antidepressant (sertraline) should not be stopped abruptly. Plan a review at 3 months — if mood has improved with thyroid treatment, a supervised taper can be planned at that point.

"I want to be honest — I think your thyroid is the primary driver of your low mood. We should treat that first and then review whether the antidepressant is still needed at 3 months. We won't stop it suddenly."
💕
Relationships and Intimacy

Hypothyroid fatigue, low libido, and mood changes place significant strain on intimate relationships. Partners may interpret the patient's withdrawal, low energy, and emotional flattening as loss of interest or depression — creating relationship conflict on top of an undiagnosed illness.

After diagnosis, some patients and partners benefit from a simple explanation of what hypothyroidism does to energy, mood, and libido — and realistic reassurance that these changes are typically reversible with treatment.

Low libido from hypothyroidism should not be attributed to relationship problems without biochemical investigation. TSH should be one of the first tests in any patient presenting with new reduced libido or sexual dysfunction.

"Some of the changes you've noticed in your energy and mood — and in how you've been in your relationship — are very likely to improve as the thyroid treatment takes effect. It can take a few months, but the improvement is often quite marked."
🤰
Fertility and Preconception

In this case, the patient is trying to conceive. Untreated hypothyroidism causes anovulatory cycles, menorrhagia, and increased miscarriage risk. The TSH must be optimised to <2.5 before conception — this is the most urgent priority in this consultation.

Framing this positively — "treating the thyroid significantly improves the chances of a successful pregnancy" — is honest and motivating. This is not a minor incidental finding; it is the single most clinically important aspect of the management plan for this patient.

Refer to obstetric endocrinology simultaneously with starting treatment — do not wait until pregnant to involve the specialist team.

"Treating your thyroid will significantly improve your chances of getting pregnant and having a healthy pregnancy. This is the most important thing we can do for you right now, and I want to act on it today."
💊
Lifelong Medication Anxiety

Many patients are anxious about committing to lifelong daily medication. Hashimoto's hypothyroidism is almost always permanent — this is a lifelong condition requiring lifelong treatment. Normalising this honestly ("it's similar to taking a blood pressure tablet or vitamin D — it becomes part of your daily routine") reduces anxiety.

Emphasising the simplicity of the regimen — one tablet daily, inexpensive, well-tolerated when dose is right — contrasts with the complexity of managing the undiagnosed condition (multiple GP visits, antidepressants, investigations for individual symptoms).

Address the fear of side effects directly: levothyroxine is bio-identical to the body's own T4 — when the dose is correct, there are essentially no side effects beyond the consequences of over-replacement (dose too high).

"This tablet is replacing something your body needs but isn't making enough of — it's bio-identical to your own hormone. When the dose is right, most people have no side effects at all. It becomes as simple as taking a vitamin every morning."

7J — Safety-netting: exact phrases + medico-legal rationale

⚠ Three scenario-specific safety-net phrases

🔴 Emergency — myxoedema features
"If at any point before your blood tests — or in the future if you ever become very unwell — you develop confusion, a very low body temperature where you feel profoundly cold, or you are very difficult to rouse, please call 999 immediately. That combination of symptoms can indicate that the thyroid has become severely underactive and needs emergency treatment."
Myxoedema coma has a mortality of 25–60%. Precipitants (cold exposure, infection, opioids) can cause acute decompensation in a patient with chronic hypothyroidism. This safety-net, documented in the notes, is particularly important for patients with severe or rapidly progressive hypothyroidism. It also creates a medico-legal record that the patient was counselled on the emergency features.
💊 Medication — critical timing and interaction
"The most important thing about this tablet is when you take it. Please take it first thing in the morning on an empty stomach, and wait 30 minutes before eating. Your calcium tablet must be taken at a completely different time — at least 4 hours later, such as at lunchtime. If you take them together, the calcium blocks the absorption and the tablet will not work properly — and we might think the dose needs to go up when actually the problem is timing."
The calcium/levothyroxine interaction is the most common cause of apparent levothyroxine treatment failure in primary care. Documenting that the patient was counselled on specific timing is essential — if TSH does not normalise at 6 weeks, you can ask "are you separating them by 4 hours?" before escalating the dose. This counselling is both clinically important and protects against unnecessary dose escalation and over-treatment.
🤰 Pregnancy — immediate action required
"Because you are trying to conceive, there is one thing I need you to do the moment you get a positive pregnancy test — before anything else. Call us the same day and tell us you are pregnant and you are on levothyroxine. We will immediately increase your dose and arrange an urgent blood test. Do not wait for a routine appointment — this needs to happen on day one of a positive test."
The foetus depends entirely on maternal T4 for neurological development until 16–20 weeks. A delay of even a few weeks in identifying pregnancy and increasing levothyroxine dosing can result in foetal neurodevelopmental impairment. This is one of the most time-critical safety-nets in all of primary care — it must be given verbally, written down for the patient, and documented in the notes. It is a medico-legal imperative.
Why safety-netting matters beyond clinical care
Calcium/levothyroxine interaction counselling documented — if TSH does not normalise at 6 weeks, the interaction was addressed; dose escalation without this counselling first is premature
Pregnancy safety-net documented — if foetal thyroid complication occurs, documented counselling of immediate dose increase on positive test is essential medico-legal protection
Myxoedema safety-net — patients with chronic hypothyroidism risk acute decompensation; documented awareness of emergency features is part of safe management
Do not titrate by symptoms alone — this must be documented in every levothyroxine management note; TSH must be rechecked before dose change
Annual TSH monitoring documented — hypothyroidism is a lifelong condition requiring annual biochemical review; missing annual monitoring creates medico-legal risk if complications develop

💊 3 — Levothyroxine Dosing Quick Reference
Initiation and Titration
Standard: 50 mcg OD fasting → TSH at 6–8 weeks → titrate ↑25 mcg until TSH 0.5–2.5
Elderly/cardiac: 25 mcg OD → very slow titration; ECG before; accept TSH 1.0–4.0
Pregnancy: 50–75 mcg; TSH target <2.5; recheck every 4 weeks; ↑25% on positive test
Never: titrate by symptoms alone; always wait for 6-week TSH before changing dose
⚠ Calcium, iron, PPIs reduce absorption by 20–40% — minimum 4 hours separation
TSH Not Responding — Check First
1. Adherence — missed doses, irregular timing
2. Drug interaction — calcium/iron/PPI taken with levothyroxine
3. Malabsorption — coeliac disease (check anti-tTG)
4. Dose too low for weight — recalculate (~1.6 mcg/kg IBW)
5. Biotin supplementation — can falsely alter TSH assay
Then: consider endocrine referral if all excluded
