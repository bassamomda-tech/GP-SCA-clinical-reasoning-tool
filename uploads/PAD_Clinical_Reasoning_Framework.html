📋 Clinical Stem — Leg Pain & Walking Difficulty in Primary Care
A patient presents with exertional leg pain and reduced walking distance — what should you do?
Mr Derek Hargreaves, 64, attends the surgery complaining of a cramping pain in his right calf that comes on after walking about 200 metres and eases when he stops. He has smoked 20/day for 40 years, has type 2 diabetes managed with metformin, and takes ramipril for hypertension. He is worried because he read on the internet that people with blocked arteries can lose their legs. His wife is in the waiting room and is pushing him to "get it sorted."
This stem presents a classic intermittent claudication scenario, but in practice PAD presents across a wide spectrum — from incidental findings on ABPI screening to acute limb-threatening ischaemia. The framework below adapts across the following scenarios.
Scenario A — Classic Claudication 64M smoker with exertional calf pain at 200m, relieved by rest, worried about amputation. Straightforward PAD — focus on CVD risk reduction, supervised exercise, and managing expectations about prognosis.
Scenario B — Critical Limb Ischaemia 72F with rest pain in the foot at night, hanging the leg out of bed for relief, with a non-healing ulcer on the great toe. DM and CKD. Requires same-day vascular assessment — limb-threatening emergency.
Scenario C — Atypical Presentation 58M with bilateral thigh heaviness and erectile dysfunction. No classic calf claudication. Aorto-iliac disease (Leriche syndrome) may be missed if you only think of calf pain.
Scenario D — Incidental Low ABPI 68F found to have ABPI 0.82 during diabetic foot check. Asymptomatic. QRISK3 already ≥10%. How do you communicate cardiovascular risk without causing alarm?
Scenario E — Acute Limb Ischaemia 76M with AF presents with sudden onset cold, pale, pulseless right foot for 4 hours. The 6 Ps are present. This is a surgical emergency — 999 and do not delay.
Key variables to adapt for Smoking status (current vs ex vs never); diabetes and CKD (accelerate disease, complicate ABPI); ethnicity (higher prevalence in South Asian and Afro-Caribbean populations); pre-existing CVD; walking capacity and functional limitation; patient's understanding and fear of amputation; rest pain vs claudication only.

Step 1
History Taking — Open Question First · Targeted Questions · ICE · Psychosocial Context
▲collapse
History is the single highest-yield component of the PAD consultation. A structured history differentiates intermittent claudication from critical limb ischaemia, identifies the cardiovascular risk burden, screens for acute ischaemia, and — crucially — uncovers the psychosocial fear (usually amputation) that dominates the patient's agenda unless you name it first.
🎓 Consultation opener — use existing information first
"Good morning Mr Hargreaves. I can see from your notes you've come in about a pain in your leg when you walk. Before I ask anything else, could you tell me in your own words what's been happening?"
The case card already states the presenting complaint and key comorbidities. Asking "What brings you in today?" when the stem has told you wastes time and signals to the examiner you haven't read the brief — costing marks in Global Skills.

🛡️

Safeguarding Considerations — Consider in Every Consultation

PAD predominantly affects older adults with multiple comorbidities — a population vulnerable to neglect, carer abuse, and self-neglect. Non-healing ulcers in a patient who "didn't notice" may reflect neglect. Inability to attend appointments may indicate coercive control or carer failure.
🏠 Domestic Abuse / Intimate Partner Violence
  • Controlling partner may restrict access to GP, pharmacy, or exercise programmes
  • Financial abuse may prevent purchase of appropriate footwear or heating
  • Partner minimises symptoms: "he's just lazy, he doesn't want to walk"
  • Unexplained injuries attributed to "poor circulation" or "tripping due to numb feet"
👴 Older Adults / Carer-related Concern
  • Neglected foot care — long toenails, fungal infections, pressure sores unattended
  • Malnutrition accelerating wound healing failure and vascular disease
  • Carer not administering medications — statins, antiplatelets stopped
  • Social isolation preventing attendance at supervised exercise or vascular appointments
🧒 Children in the Household
  • Patient with CLI/amputation may be sole carer for grandchildren — assess coping
  • Smoking in the home with children — opportunity for whole-household cessation
  • Young carer role — child providing wound care or mobility assistance
  • Parental disability may impact school attendance if child provides care
💊 Self-Harm / Medication Misuse Risk
  • Chronic CLI pain may lead to opioid dependence or misuse
  • Diagnosis of "blocked arteries" or threat of amputation can trigger depression and suicidal ideation
  • Non-concordance as passive self-harm — "what's the point?"
  • Alcohol excess as coping mechanism — accelerates disease, interacts with anticoagulants
If a safeguarding concern is identified: Document concerns clearly using direct quotes. Discuss with safeguarding lead. For adults at risk, consider referral to adult social care under the Care Act 2014. For children, follow local MASH procedures. Do not delay vascular referral — both pathways run in parallel.

1D — ICE: Ideas · Concerns · Expectations — in every consultation, not just SCA
💡 Why ICE matters in PAD — not a tick-box exercise

Patients with PAD often present with a hidden catastrophic fear — amputation — that they may not volunteer. If you don't name this fear and address it directly, your management plan will fall on deaf ears. The patient who believes they are going to lose their leg will not hear your advice about statins and supervised exercise. ICE is the gateway to concordance: once you understand the patient's model, you can reframe the prognosis with real numbers.

💭 Ideas
"What do you think might be causing this pain? Have you had any thoughts about what's going on?"
Many patients have already self-diagnosed via the internet. Their model may range from "just cramp" to "gangrene." Understanding this lets you tailor your explanation — trivial models mean they won't take treatment seriously; catastrophic models mean they're too anxious to plan.
😟 Concerns
"I want to understand what's worrying you most. Some people with leg pain worry about very serious things — is there anything specific on your mind?"
The unspoken fear is almost always amputation. By naming the possibility before the patient does, you give them permission to voice it. Once named, you can address it with evidence: "Most people with your level of circulation problem do NOT lose their leg."
🎯 Expectations
"What were you hoping we might be able to do about this today? Were you expecting a scan, medication, or something else?"
Patients may expect an immediate scan, a bypass, or simply reassurance. If their expectation is a scan and you explain ABPI comes first, they feel heard. If they expected a pill and you explain exercise is most effective, skilled negotiation bridges the gap.
1E — Psychosocial context: the person behind the peripheral arterial disease
🫂 PAD is a disease of accumulated vascular damage — shaped by decades of social context

Peripheral arterial disease is the end product of decades of smoking, metabolic syndrome driven by food poverty, untreated hypertension in populations with poor healthcare access, and depression that reduces motivation to exercise. The psychosocial history determines whether the patient can access supervised exercise, afford the bus to the vascular clinic, or muster the motivation to quit smoking.

🚬 Smoking Identity & Quit Readiness

After 40 years, smoking is woven into identity, social network, and coping strategy. Abrupt "you must stop" rarely works; motivational interviewing is more effective.

"I can see you've smoked a long time. Can you tell me what smoking means to you day-to-day — is it something you enjoy, or feel stuck with?"

Quit-ready → varenicline + structured support. Pre-contemplative → harm reduction, revisit at every contact.

🏠 Social Isolation & Mobility Loss

Claudication shrinks the patient's world. They stop walking to shops, visiting friends, attending clubs. Isolation accelerates depression and reduces exercise — the primary treatment.

"How has this leg pain changed what you're able to do day-to-day? Are there things you've had to give up?"

If isolated → social prescribing, adapted walking groups, community transport to exercise programme.

💼 Occupational Impact & Financial Stress

PAD disproportionately affects manual workers. A bricklayer with 100m claudication faces job loss. Financial stress compounds depression and reduces adherence.

"Is this affecting your ability to work? Are you worried about your income because of this?"

Fit note, occupational health, Citizens Advice for benefits, Access to Work scheme.

😔 Depression & Health Anxiety

PAD + depression halves exercise adherence, doubles smoking relapse, increases CV mortality by 30%. Amputation threat triggers disabling health anxiety.

"Living with this can take a toll on mood. How have you been feeling — have you been feeling low or anxious?"

PHQ-2 positive → full PHQ-9; SSRI (sertraline safe in CVD); IAPT referral.

🍽️ Diet, Poverty & Health Literacy

Mediterranean diet reduces CV events by 30% (PREDIMED), but requires access to fresh food and cooking skills. Patients in food poverty eat processed, high-sodium diets.

"Can I ask about what you eat day to day? I know healthy eating can be difficult depending on circumstances."

Food poverty → food bank signposting, social prescribing, cooking skills programmes.

👥 Carer Burden & Family Dynamics

The patient may be carer for a spouse with dementia or disabled child. Their own health is deprioritised. Alternatively, the spouse may be driving the consultation.

"Is there anyone at home who depends on you? Sometimes people put their own health last."

Carer → carer's assessment, respite care; spouse-driven → explore patient's own priorities.

Step 2
Triage Engine — Emergency · Urgent · Routine
▲collapse
Triage in PAD spans a wide acuity range. Acute limb ischaemia is one of the most time-critical vascular emergencies (6 hours to irreversible damage). Critical limb ischaemia needs urgent vascular assessment. Intermittent claudication — the most common presentation — is managed in primary care with cardiovascular risk reduction and supervised exercise.
🔴 Emergency

999 or Same-Day Hospital

Call 999 / A&E now
  • Acute limb ischaemia (6 Ps)Cold, pale, pulseless limb — 6-hour window. 999 to vascular surgery.
  • Wet gangrene / spreading infectionNecrotic tissue with purulence, systemic sepsis. Emergency debridement + IV antibiotics.
  • Acute compartment syndromeTense, swollen calf post-reperfusion. Pain out of proportion. Fasciotomy within hours.
  • Ruptured popliteal aneurysmAcute ischaemia from aneurysm thrombosis — emergency vascular surgery.
  • Concurrent acute coronary syndromePAD patient with chest pain — cardiac emergency takes priority.
🟠 Urgent

Same-Day GP / Urgent Referral

Days to 2 weeks
  • Rest pain (CLI — Fontaine III)ABPI <0.5, forefoot pain at night. Urgent vascular referral within 2 weeks.
  • Non-healing ischaemic ulcer (Fontaine IV)Tissue loss on toes/heel. 2-week wait vascular referral.
  • Rapidly deteriorating claudicationWalking distance halved in <4 weeks — acute plaque event. Urgent assessment.
  • Blue toe syndromeAtheroembolic shower — urgent assessment to identify proximal source.
  • Dry gangrene (localised, no infection)Urgent referral — assess for revascularisation potential.
  • Suspected popliteal aneurysmPulsatile mass behind knee — urgent duplex + vascular review.
🟢 Routine

Manage in Primary Care

GP practice
  • Stable intermittent claudicationDistance stable, no rest pain. CVD risk reduction + supervised exercise.
  • Incidental low ABPI (0.5–0.9)Asymptomatic PAD. Cardiovascular risk factor optimisation.
  • Mild claudication >200m, improvingResponding to exercise. Continue primary care management.
  • Post-intervention surveillanceStable post-angioplasty/bypass — annual ABPI, foot checks, CVD review.
  • Lifestyle-limiting claudication <200mCVD management + supervised exercise; vascular referral if no improvement after 3 months.

Step 5
Reaching a Diagnosis & DDx — Explained in Plain Language
▲collapse
Explaining PAD in plain language is one of the hardest communication challenges in general practice. The patient needs to understand their arteries are narrowed, this is the same process causing heart attacks, amputation is NOT inevitable, and the most effective treatment is exercise. If you use jargon, the patient hears nothing after "disease."
🗣️ Explaining the Diagnosis in Plain Language — say something like this

"What's happening is that the blood vessels in your legs have become narrower over time — a bit like old plumbing getting furred up. When you walk and your muscles need more blood, they can't get enough, and that's what causes the cramp. The important thing is that this is the same process that can affect the heart and brain, so we need to look after your whole circulation. The really good news is that for most people, the leg doesn't get worse — and with treatment, we can actually improve how far you walk. The best treatment, believe it or not, is a structured walking programme — it helps your body grow new small blood vessels around the blockage."

💬 Addressing the patient's own explanation — why it may not be the full picture

"I just thought it was cramp from getting older."
"It's understandable to think that. But cramp that comes on at a predictable distance and goes away when you stop is a specific pattern telling us the blood supply isn't quite keeping up. It's worth checking because there are treatments that make a real difference."

"I read online that people with blocked arteries lose their legs."
"I can understand why that's frightening. For most people with your level of circulation problem, the risk of losing your leg is actually very low — around 1–2% per year. What matters more is your heart and brain health, because the same furring affects those too. That's why we treat the whole picture."

7C — Non-medication management: mechanism + evidence + tailored advice
Never give generic lifestyle advice. In PAD, lifestyle interventions have specific vascular mechanisms. Supervised exercise grows collateral vessels. Smoking cessation halves progression. Mediterranean diet reduces CV events 30%. Each piece of advice must name the mechanism, evidence, and practical action.
🚶
Supervised Exercise Programme
2hr/week × 3 months (NICE NG224)
Mechanism

Repeated ischaemia-reperfusion stimulates angiogenesis — new collateral vessels around stenosis. Improves endothelial function and muscle oxygen extraction.

Practical

Refer to NHS supervised exercise programme. Walk until moderate pain (3/5), rest, repeat 30–45 min. Walking INTO the pain is the treatment — reassure this is safe.

Walking distance ↑ 50–200% (Cochrane 2017)
🚭
Smoking Cessation
Complete cessation
Mechanism

Nicotine causes vasoconstriction, platelet aggregation, endothelial damage. CO reduces oxygen capacity. Continued smoking triples amputation risk.

Practical

Offer varenicline + behavioural support. Set quit date within 2 weeks. NRT as alternative. Vaping as harm reduction. Follow up 2, 4, 12 weeks.

Amputation risk ↓ 3-fold; CV events ↓ 36%
🥗
Mediterranean Diet
↑ fish, olive oil, veg, nuts · ↓ processed
Mechanism

Anti-inflammatory pattern reduces oxidative stress and endothelial dysfunction. Omega-3 has anti-thrombotic properties. High fibre improves glycaemic control.

Practical

Oily fish 2×/week. Cook with olive oil. Handful unsalted nuts daily. 5+ fruit/veg. Reduce processed meat. Whole grains over white.

CV events ↓ 30% (PREDIMED)
🦶
Foot Care
Daily inspection + podiatry
Mechanism

Ischaemic feet heal poorly. Minor trauma progresses to ulcers, infection, gangrene. Diabetic neuropathy means patient may not feel injury.

Practical

Daily inspection (mirror for soles). Wash/dry between toes. Never walk barefoot. Professionally fitted shoes. Podiatry for nails. Report any colour/temp change immediately.

Prevents up to 50% of amputations
⚖️
Weight Management
BMI 20–25 or 5–10% loss
Mechanism

Obesity worsens insulin resistance, raises BP and LDL, mechanically impairs walking. Weight loss reduces all four pathways.

Practical

5–10% loss is meaningful. Combine with supervised exercise. Consider GLP-1 if BMI ≥35 + T2DM. Sustainable changes, not crash diets.

↓ BP 5–10mmHg per 5% weight loss
🍺
Alcohol Moderation
<14 units/week, ≥3 days spread
Mechanism

Excess alcohol raises BP, contributes to AF (embolic risk), causes weight gain, interacts with anticoagulants. Thiamine deficiency → neuropathy.

Practical

AUDIT-C screen. >14u/week → brief intervention. If dependent → specialist services. Count units explicitly. Alcohol-free days alone insufficient.

↓ BP 2–4mmHg; ↓ AF risk; ↓ bleeding risk
7D — Drug selector: prescribing guide for PAD
Three pillars of best medical therapy: antiplatelet (clopidogrel 75mg), high-intensity statin (atorvastatin 80mg), and BP control (ACEi/ARB first-line). Start all three at diagnosis — do not wait for vascular referral.
Antiplatelet Therapy
First-line: Clopidogrel 75mg OD

NICE NG224: clopidogrel preferred over aspirin for PAD. CAPRIE trial showed 8.7% relative risk reduction vs aspirin for vascular events in PAD subgroup.

Alternative: Aspirin 75mg OD if clopidogrel contraindicated or not tolerated.

Dual antiplatelet: Not routine in PAD. COMPASS trial (rivaroxaban 2.5mg BD + aspirin) showed benefit in stable atherosclerotic disease — specialist decision only.

⚠️ If on anticoagulant (AF): discuss with vascular team before adding antiplatelet — bleeding risk
Statin Therapy
Atorvastatin 80mg OD (secondary prevention)

PAD = established atherosclerotic CVD → secondary prevention intensity. Heart Protection Study: simvastatin reduced CV events 22% in PAD subgroup regardless of baseline cholesterol.

If intolerant: Try lower dose (atorvastatin 20mg) → rosuvastatin → alternate-day dosing. True statin intolerance is rare (~5%). Re-challenge after washout.

Target: Non-HDL cholesterol reduction ≥40% from baseline. Recheck at 3 months.

⚠️ Check LFTs at baseline and 3 months. CK only if symptomatic.
Blood Pressure Control
Target: <140/90 mmHg (ACEi/ARB first-line)

ACE inhibitor (ramipril) or ARB has additional vascular protective effects beyond BP reduction (HOPE trial). Amlodipine second-line.

Caution: Avoid over-aggressive BP lowering in severe PAD — already compromised perfusion. Do not reduce systolic below 110mmHg.

If diabetic: BP target <140/90 same. Check U&E 2 weeks after ACEi start. eGFR drop >25% → stop and investigate renal artery stenosis.

⚠️ eGFR drop >25% on ACEi → consider renal artery stenosis (common in PAD patients)

🏥 Clinic Quick-Reference Card — PAD

Diagnosis

ABPI ≤0.9 confirms PAD. 0.5–0.9 = claudication. <0.5 = CLI. >1.3 = calcified (unreliable in DM/CKD → toe pressures). Classic claudication: predictable distance, calf pain, relieved 2–5 min rest.

Emergency Red Flags

Acute ischaemia (6 Ps) → 999. Rest pain → urgent vascular 2ww. Non-healing ulcer/gangrene → urgent vascular. Blue toe → urgent. Rapid deterioration → urgent.

Investigations (GP-initiated)

ABPI, FBC, HbA1c, lipid profile, U&E/eGFR, ECG, QRISK3. Duplex USS and advanced imaging = vascular team decision.

Best Medical Therapy — Start at Diagnosis

Antiplatelet: Clopidogrel 75mg OD (NICE first-line). Aspirin 75mg if intolerant.
Statin: Atorvastatin 80mg OD (secondary prevention). Target non-HDL ↓≥40%.
BP: ACEi first-line, target <140/90. Check U&E at 2 weeks.
Smoking: Varenicline + behavioural support. Single most effective intervention.

Exercise

Supervised exercise programme: 2hr/week × 3 months (NICE NG224). Walk INTO moderate pain, rest, repeat. Improves walking distance 50–200%. Naftidrofuryl only if exercise unavailable.

Referral Thresholds

Lifestyle-limiting <200m after 3m exercise → vascular referral. CLI (rest pain, tissue loss, ABPI <0.5) → 2-week urgent. Acute ischaemia → 999. Popliteal aneurysm → urgent duplex + referral.

Follow-Up Timeline

2 weeks (U&E), 4 weeks (meds/BP/ABPI review), 3 months (lipids/exercise completion), 6 months (CVD review), then annual.

Key Numbers

Amputation risk 1–2%/year in claudication. 5-year CV mortality 20–30%. Statin ↓ CV events 25%. Exercise ↑ distance 50–200%. Cessation ↓ amputation 3-fold.
