Why the 6–8 Week Check Matters

The 6–8 week postnatal consultation is a key moment to assess maternal physical and mental health, review infant development, and provide health promotion. NICE quality statement 6 states that every woman should have a GP assessment 6–8 weeks after giving birth.

🔴 Crucial components – Mother

  • Mental health screen (EPDS/PHQ‑9)
  • Physical recovery (perineum, CS scar, lochia)
  • Contraception & family planning
  • Pre‑existing/pregnancy‑related conditions (diabetes, hypertension)
  • Health promotion (feeding, smoking, safe sleep)

🔵 Crucial components – Baby

  • Growth & general appearance
  • Head‑to‑toe physical examination (NIPE: eyes, heart, hips, testes)
  • Development & social smiling
  • Feeding, immunisations, parental concerns
  • Abnormal findings & red flags
💡 Key principleThis is a holistic consultation, not a tick‑box exercise. Tailor the discussion to the woman’s individual experience and needs. “What Good Looks Like” (NHS England, 2023) emphasises a personalised, trauma‑informed approach.
👩

Mother Check – Stepwise Assessment

StepDomainWhat to Ask & Assess
1 Mental Health & Wellbeing
  • Use open questions: “How has your mood been since the birth?”
  • Screen for postnatal depression (EPDS or PHQ‑9).
  • Ask about anxiety, bonding, and traumatic birth experiences.
  • If red flags (self‑harm, psychosis) → urgent perinatal mental health referral.
2 Physical Recovery
  • Perineal healing / CS scar – any ongoing pain or concerns?
  • Vaginal loss: lochia should have ceased; ask about offensive discharge or heavy bleeding.
  • Bladder & bowel: urinary/faecal incontinence, pelvic pressure (prolapse).
  • BP check – especially if hypertension during pregnancy.
  • Offer referral to perinatal pelvic health service if needed.
3 Contraception & Family Planning
  • Discuss options (LARC can be fitted from 3–6 weeks).
  • Advise that ovulation can occur before the first period, even if breastfeeding.
4 Pre‑existing / Pregnancy‑Related Conditions
  • Gestational diabetes: Arrange HbA1c at 6–13 weeks postpartum & refer to Diabetes Prevention Programme.
  • Hypertensive disorders: Ensure BP has normalised; if not, investigate for chronic hypertension.
  • Anaemia / thyroid: Check FBC, TFTs if indicated.
  • BMI ≥30: Offer weight management advice.
5 Health Promotion & Lifestyle
  • Infant feeding: encourage breastfeeding; vitamin D for breastfeeding mothers.
  • Cervical screening: reschedule for 12 weeks postpartum if due.
  • MMR vaccination: check status; offer if needed (avoid pregnancy for 1 month).
  • Smoking, alcohol, substance use: brief advice.
  • Safe sleeping: reinforce “Back to Sleep” and smoke‑free environment.
⚠ Trauma‑informed careMany women have experienced a difficult or traumatic birth. Ask sensitively and offer referral to perinatal mental health or pelvic health services when needed.
👶

Baby Check – Stepwise Assessment

StepDomainWhat to Assess & Key Actions
1 Growth & General Appearance Weight & head circumference → plot in Red Book. Observe colour, breathing, activity, posture. Jaundice, pallor, cyanosis.
2 Head‑to‑Toe Physical Examination
EyesRed reflex, opacities, squint, fixing & following
HeartPosition, murmurs, femoral pulses, heart rate
LungsRespiratory effort, rate, added sounds
HipsBarlow & Ortolani, symmetrical skin creases
Genitalia (boys)Testicular descent, hypospadias
SpineInspect & palpate; skin integrity

Also: head/fontanelles, face, palate, ears, neck, clavicles, limbs, hands/feet, abdomen, skin, CNS (tone, movements).

💡 NIPE reminder: The infant screening exam specifically targets eyes, heart, hips, and testes (the four NIPE conditions).
3 Development & Social Smiling Social smiling (usually present by 6–8 weeks). Visual fixing & following. Head control (some lag when pulled to sit). Tone – should not be stiff or floppy.
4 Feeding, Immunisations & Parental Concerns Ask about feeding (breast, formula, mixed). Discuss 8‑week immunisations (arrange with practice nurse). Address parental worries – crying, sleep, rashes, bowel habits.

Common Abnormal Findings & Actions (Baby)

Use this quick‑reference table during the examination. Findings are grouped by urgency, based on NIPE and NICE guidance.

SystemAbnormal FindingAction
Eyes Absent / abnormal red reflex, white pupil (leukocoria) Urgent Same‑day paediatric ophthalmology referral
Heart Loud murmur (≥3/6), diastolic murmur, absent femoral pulses, cyanosis, sweating with feeds Urgent Immediate paediatric cardiology / echo
Hips Positive Barlow or Ortolani (dislocatable/dislocated hip), significant leg length difference Urgent Urgent paediatric orthopaedics / hip USS
Testes Bilateral non‑palpable testes (possible DSD) Urgent Same‑day paediatric endocrinology/urology
Neurology Marked hypotonia/floppiness or hypertonia, persistent fisting, significant asymmetry Urgent Immediate paediatric neurology assessment
Growth/General Weight loss >10% birth weight not regained, severe jaundice (after 2 wks), apnoeic episodes Urgent Paediatric admission
Heart Soft (≤2/6) ejection systolic murmur, normal pulses, no symptoms Routine Document; review at 8‑week checks or refer if persists >6 months
Hips Clicky hips only (no instability); asymmetric skin creases but normal exam Routine Reassure; consider hip USS if breech/FHx; routine HV follow‑up
Testes Unilateral undescended testis (palpable in inguinal canal) Routine Non‑urgent paediatric surgical referral by 3–6 months
Head Flat occiput (positional plagiocephaly), small anterior fontanelle but well‑grown Routine Positioning advice (tummy time); review in 4–6 weeks
Skin Erythema toxicum, milia, Mongolian blue spots, capillary naevus Routine Reassure; no action unless changing/symptomatic
Eyes Intermittent convergent squint (up to 2 months) or sticky eye without redness Routine Explain normal transient finding; if persistent >8 wks, refer ophthalmology
💡 If in doubtDiscuss with a senior colleague or arrange an early review (2–4 weeks). Document clearly in the Red Book and clinical notes.

Red Flags – Immediate Action Required

👩 Mother

Mental health: Thoughts of self‑harm, hopelessness, confusion, agitation, psychotic symptoms → urgent perinatal mental health referral.
Physical: Heavy vaginal bleeding, offensive discharge, fever, calf swelling/redness (DVT), chest pain/breathlessness (PE), severe headache with visual disturbance (pre‑eclampsia).
Pelvic floor: Inability to control urine/stool, sensation of a bulge in the vagina → refer to pelvic health service.

👶 Baby

  • Feels very stiff or very floppy.
  • Holds arms/legs in fixed positions; difficult to change nappy/clothes.
  • Repetitive jerky or writhing movements.
  • Not bringing hands to face; hands remain fisted.
  • Cannot lift head when lying on tummy.
  • Moves one side of the body much more than the other.
  • No social smiling or visual following by 8 weeks.
  • Breathlessness or sweating during feeds; blue/pale/ashen skin.
  • Yellow whites of the eyes (jaundice after 2 weeks).

ANY of the above → urgent same‑day referral or admission as appropriate

Referral Pathways – Emergency, Urgent & Routine

PriorityPathwayCriteriaAction
EMERGENCY 999 / A&E / Maternity triage Active major bleeding, suspected sepsis, severe pre‑eclampsia, perinatal psychosis with risk to self/baby, suspected NAI requiring immediate protection, acute neurological deterioration in baby. Immediate hospital admission. Do not delay for investigations.
URGENT (within 1‑2 weeks) Perinatal mental health, pelvic health, paediatric cardiology/orthopaedics Significant postnatal depression not responding to primary care management, pelvic floor dysfunction affecting daily life, abnormal NIPE finding (red reflex, hip, heart murmur) that is not immediately life‑threatening. Urgent referral. Initiate primary care management while waiting (e.g. counselling, pelvic floor exercises).
ROUTINE Health visitor, contraception clinic, physiotherapy, routine paediatrics Mild/moderate symptoms, routine contraceptive needs, positional talipes/plagiocephaly, minor feeding concerns, and all well‑baby checks with normal findings. Refer routinely or manage in primary care with safety‑netting.
