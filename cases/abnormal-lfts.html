📋 Clinical Stem — Abnormal LFT Result in Primary Care
A patient presents to discuss an abnormal liver function test result found incidentally or on routine screening.
"Mr Patel, a 52-year-old finance manager, attends to discuss blood results taken three weeks ago by the practice nurse during a health check. His ALT is 87 U/L (normal <45), his GGT is 112 U/L, and his ALP is 140 U/L. He mentions his GP 'just said the liver tests were a bit off' but did not explain what that meant. He drinks 'maybe 20 units a week' and his BMI is 31. He is on atorvastatin 40 mg, metformin 500 mg twice daily, and ibuprofen PRN for back pain. He appears well and has no jaundice."
This stem represents the most common LFT scenario in UK general practice: the incidental or screening abnormality requiring pattern recognition, systematic workup, and patient-centred explanation. The same consultation structure applies whether the elevation is mild and likely benign, or severe and suggesting liver failure. Adapt the urgency and management based on the pattern and severity of derangement.
Scenario A — MASLD/Metabolic Middle-aged, overweight patient, mild ALT/GGT elevation, metabolic risk factors (T2DM, hypertension, dyslipidaemia), no alcohol excess — classic MASLD presentation requiring lifestyle intervention and fibrosis assessment with FIB-4.
Scenario B — Alcohol-related Patient with high alcohol intake, raised GGT disproportionate to ALT, AST:ALT ratio >2:1, macrocytosis, thrombocytopenia — ARLD requiring AUDIT-C, fibrosis staging, and sensitive brief intervention without moralising.
Scenario C — Drug-induced (DILI) Patient started on a new medication (statin, antibiotics, herbal remedies) 4–12 weeks ago with new hepatocellular or cholestatic pattern — requires RUCAM causality assessment, drug cessation decision, and monitoring.
Scenario D — Cholestatic pattern Raised ALP and GGT with normal or mildly raised ALT in an older patient, possibly with pruritus — primary biliary cholangitis (especially women), gallstone disease, or pancreatic malignancy must be excluded urgently with USS.
Scenario E — Acute hepatitis Unwell patient, ALT >10× ULN, jaundice, RUQ pain — viral hepatitis (A, B, E), acute alcoholic hepatitis, or acute DILI requiring same-day assessment and possible emergency admission.
Key variables to adapt for: Degree of elevation (mild <3×, moderate 3–10×, severe >10× ULN), pattern (hepatocellular vs cholestatic vs mixed vs isolated bilirubin), symptoms (well vs unwell), alcohol intake, medication list including herbals, risk factors for viral hepatitis, BMI and metabolic syndrome, family history of liver disease.

History Taking — Open Question First · Targeted Questions · ICE · Psychosocial Context
▲collapse
The consultation begins before the question is asked. The patient has received a result they may not understand, and their anxieties about what it means — cancer, cirrhosis, the need to stop drinking — will shape everything. Acknowledge the result exists, let them speak first, then systematically characterise the pattern: hepatocellular vs cholestatic, severity, likely aetiology, and whether the patient is currently well or deteriorating. Every question must serve the differential or change the management plan.
🎓 Consultation opener — use existing information first
"I can see you have come to discuss your blood results — the liver tests that came back a few weeks ago. Before I go through them, I would love to hear from you first: what has been on your mind since you got that message?"
Opening with the patient's frame of reference scores Relating to Others (agenda-setting) and Global Skills (structure) before any clinical questioning. Asking what they already know avoids insulting patients who have researched it, and identifies catastrophising that needs addressing. Asking for their results again without signalling you have read them costs 1–2 RO marks immediately.

Safeguarding Considerations — Consider in Every Consultation

Abnormal LFTs can be a marker of harm. Alcohol-related liver disease may reflect harmful alcohol use that conceals domestic violence, financial abuse, or exploitation. In young patients, raised LFTs with vague histories should prompt enquiry about substance misuse in a safeguarding context, including coercion by adults in positions of trust.
🏠 Domestic Abuse / Intimate Partner Violence
  • Harmful alcohol use is both a risk factor for and consequence of domestic abuse
  • Ask sensitively about home circumstances if alcohol-related liver disease is suspected
  • Unexplained physical injuries with LFT abnormality may conceal physical abuse
  • Use HARK or HITS validated screening tools if concern arises
👴 Older Adults / Carer-related Concern
  • Alcohol misuse in older adults is frequently missed and may represent self-neglect or isolation
  • An older adult with abnormal LFTs and unexplained weight loss — consider financial exploitation and medication mismanagement by carers
  • Consider capacity assessment if patient is confused and has jaundice — hepatic encephalopathy impairs decision-making
  • Malnutrition (low albumin) with raised LFTs may indicate neglect rather than primary liver disease
🧒 Children in the Household
  • Significant alcohol-related liver disease in a parent requires consideration of the impact on dependent children
  • Parental alcohol use disorder is a recognised child protection risk factor — Children Act 1989 duties apply
  • Hepatitis B or C diagnosis in a parent necessitates referral of household contacts for testing and vaccination
  • Consider referral to social care if chaotic alcohol use with young children in the household
💊 Self-Harm / Substance Misuse Risk
  • Paracetamol overdose (self-harm) causes acute liver failure — always ask about overdose in young patients with acute elevated transaminases
  • IVDU-related hepatitis C — explore whether the patient is in contact with addiction services; needle exchange referral may be appropriate
  • Concealed alcohol dependence may present with LFT abnormality without disclosure — use AUDIT-C and CAGE
  • Prescribed medication misuse can cause DILI
If a safeguarding concern is identified: Document clearly in the medical record, avoid dismissing concerns because the patient denies harm, discuss with the safeguarding lead or named nurse, and consider referral to children's or adult social care as appropriate. Your duty of care to any identified third party may override patient confidentiality under the Children Act 1989 and the Care Act 2014.

1D — ICE: Ideas · Concerns · Expectations — in every consultation, not just SCA
💡 Why ICE matters in abnormal LFTs — not a tick-box exercise

A patient told their "liver tests are abnormal" without further context will construct their own narrative — usually the worst-case one. Their Ideas (cancer? cirrhosis? the drinking has caught up with them?) will determine whether they engage with the investigation plan or disengage. Their Concerns (will they lose their job? driving licence? have to tell their family?) will determine adherence to lifestyle advice. Their Expectations (a scan? a referral? immediate treatment?) must be elicited before the plan is offered, or shared decision-making is impossible. ICE in LFT consultations also serves to uncover the alcohol story the patient is reluctant to tell.

💭 Ideas
"When you found out the liver tests were abnormal, what did you think might be causing it?"
Uncovers whether the patient has already attributed it to alcohol, medication, diet, or fears cancer. A patient who says "I thought it was probably the drinking" opens a different conversation than one who says "I have no idea, I feel completely fine" — one needs Brief Intervention, the other needs reassurance and explanation of common benign causes.
😟 Concerns
"What is your biggest worry about this result? Is there something specific you have been anxious about?"
The hidden agenda is almost always cancer, cirrhosis, or something catastrophic. Naming it — "a lot of people worry this could mean something serious, like cancer" — deflates the unspoken fear and allows a focused, reassuring response. Failing to address the hidden concern means the patient leaves with the same anxiety even if the medical plan is appropriate.
🎯 Expectations
"What were you hoping we might be able to do today — or what were you expecting might happen next?"
Some patients expect an urgent scan; others expect immediate referral; others want to be told it is nothing. Eliciting the expectation before presenting the plan allows you to validate the reasonable parts, negotiate the unreasonable parts, and avoid leaving patients feeling dismissed if their expectation is not met.
1E — Psychosocial context: the person behind the LFTs
🤝 Psychosocial factors that cause, perpetuate, and conceal liver disease

Liver disease is uniquely entangled with psychosocial context. Alcohol-related liver disease — the UK's leading cause of liver-related death in working-age adults — is driven by stress, socioeconomic deprivation, mental health comorbidity, and social isolation. MASLD is driven by food insecurity, time poverty, sedentary work, and the obesogenic environment. Understanding the patient's social world determines whether lifestyle advice is realistic or insulting, and whether treatment engagement is achievable.

🍷 Alcohol and Stress

Stress-induced alcohol use is the most common psychosocial driver of ARLD in UK primary care. High-pressure occupations (finance, law, healthcare, hospitality), bereavement, divorce, and job loss are precipitating contexts. Alcohol is often the patient's primary coping mechanism.

"A lot of people find they are drinking more when things are difficult. How have things been for you recently at home or at work?"

If stress is driving alcohol use, abstinence alone without addressing the stressor will fail. Mental health support, occupational referral, and alcohol liaison are part of the plan.

🏙️ Socioeconomic Deprivation

Liver disease shows the steepest socioeconomic gradient of any organ-specific disease in England. ARLD mortality is disproportionately higher in deprived areas; MASLD is driven by food deserts, shift work, and inability to afford gym membership or healthy food.

"I want to make sure any advice I give is actually realistic for your situation. What does your typical week look like — meals, activity, work patterns?"

Prescribe lifestyle advice that is free, accessible, and realistic. Referral to a dietitian, social prescribing link worker, or food bank signposting may be more useful than a generic diet sheet.

🧠 Mental Health Comorbidity

Depression and anxiety are bidirectionally linked with both ARLD (alcohol as self-medication) and MASLD (bidirectional neuroendocrine and inflammatory links). Antidepressants (particularly mirtazapine, tricyclics) can raise transaminases. Patients with serious mental illness have higher rates of liver disease and lower rates of investigation.

"How has your mood been recently? Some people find liver problems linked to alcohol can also affect how they are feeling emotionally."

PHQ-9 / GAD-7 scoring is appropriate. Mental health treatment is a prerequisite for successful alcohol harm reduction in many patients.

👪 Family and Social Support

Liver disease — whether alcohol-related, viral, or metabolic — affects the entire family unit. Patients with ARLD often conceal drinking from partners; hepatitis B or C diagnoses carry stigma and affect relationships, sexual activity, and partner notification. Social isolation is a key risk factor for continued harmful alcohol use.

"Is there anyone at home who knows what has been going on with these results? Do you feel you have support around you?"

Involving a supportive family member (with consent) significantly improves engagement with ARLD programmes. Hepatitis B and C partner notification is a clinical and public health duty.

🏢 Work and Occupation

ARLD in high-pressure occupations often involves corporate drinking culture. MASLD is particularly associated with sedentary desk work and long hours preventing exercise and home cooking. A diagnosis of liver disease — particularly cirrhosis — has significant occupational implications including driving (HGV/PSV licence) and some regulated professions.

"How would any changes to your health — or to your drinking — affect your work? Is drinking part of your working culture?"

Occupational health referral may be appropriate. DVLA guidance requires notification if cirrhosis with complications is confirmed — this is a doctor's duty to advise clearly.

🌎 Cultural and Ethnic Background

Hepatitis B prevalence is significantly higher in South Asian, sub-Saharan African, East Asian, and Eastern European communities due to vertical transmission and limited childhood vaccination in countries of origin. Stigma around hepatitis diagnoses in these communities can prevent disclosure and treatment. MASLD has a higher prevalence in South Asian patients at lower BMI thresholds.

"I want to make sure I understand your background. Have you or your family had any liver or hepatitis tests before, perhaps in another country?"

MASLD fibrosis risk in South Asian patients is higher at BMI >23 kg/m² (Asian-specific threshold). Hepatitis B family screening is a clinical and public health priority in affected communities.

Triage Engine — Emergency · Urgent · Routine
▲collapse
LFT abnormalities span the full triage spectrum — from a mildly raised ALT in a well patient (routine) to acute liver failure with encephalopathy (immediately life-threatening). The triage decision is primarily based on: (1) is the patient systemically well or unwell? (2) what is the degree of elevation? and (3) are there features of hepatic decompensation? Any feature of decompensation mandates same-day or emergency assessment regardless of the transaminase level.

Step 5
Reaching a Diagnosis & DDx — Explained in Plain Language
▲collapse
Most patients with mildly abnormal LFTs feel completely well — explaining why tests that "do not match how they feel" matter is the central communication challenge. The diagnosis must be shared in plain English, framed around the patient's own concerns, and accompanied by a clear explanation of what will happen if left untreated. The differential must distinguish primary care-manageable from hospital-referral diagnoses.
🗣️ Explaining the Diagnosis in Plain Language — say something like this

"Your liver is one of your body's most important organs — it processes everything you eat and drink, makes proteins that help your blood clot, and filters toxins out of your system. Think of it like a large processing factory. Your blood tests suggest the factory is under some strain — not broken, but working harder than it should be. The tests show this by measuring enzymes that leak out of liver cells when they are stressed. The important thing is that these tests can be abnormal for months or years before any symptoms appear, which is actually good news — it means we have caught this at a stage where we can still do something about it and prevent it from getting worse."

💬 Addressing the patient's own explanation — why it may not be the full picture

"I feel completely fine — surely if something was wrong I would know about it?"
"That is actually one of the trickiest things about liver problems — the liver is sometimes called the 'silent organ' because it does not cause pain until it has been under strain for a long time. By the time people feel symptoms, the damage can already be significant. The fact that we have found this early, when you feel well, means we have a real opportunity to prevent it from getting to that point."

"I only have a couple of drinks — my liver must be fine, so it must be something else."
"I hear you, and alcohol is not the only cause — there are lots of reasons liver tests can be abnormal, including the medication you are taking and your weight and blood sugar. But even moderate alcohol can affect the liver tests if other risk factors are also present, so I would like us to look at the whole picture together rather than focus on any one thing."

Diagnose & act

Step 7
Management — Expectation · Goals · Lifestyle · Prescribing · Drug Cards · Psychosocial · Follow-Up · Safety-Netting
▲collapse
Management of abnormal LFTs in primary care is fundamentally about the underlying aetiology — there is no single drug for "abnormal LFTs." Management depends on whether the cause is metabolic (MASLD — lifestyle first), alcohol-related (harm reduction and abstinence support), drug-induced (stop the offending drug), viral (antivirals), autoimmune (immunosuppression, specialist-led), or hereditary (specific treatment per condition). The GP's role across all aetiologies is the same: explain the diagnosis clearly, set realistic goals, optimise modifiable risk factors, and ensure the patient is monitored appropriately with a clear escalation plan.
7A — Address the patient's expectation first: validate → explain → negotiate
🤝
Never dismiss the expectation — acknowledge it, share your reasoning, then agree a shared plan
1
Validate — name their expectation

Most patients attend expecting either definitive reassurance ("it is nothing") or immediate intervention ("I need a scan"). Name what they expected, and acknowledge that their concern is reasonable regardless of the final outcome.

"I completely understand why you would want to know exactly what is going on — an abnormal blood test is worrying, and it makes total sense that you want answers today."
2
Explain — share your clinical reasoning

Explain why the investigation plan is staged, why you are not ordering everything today, and what the reasoning behind each test is. Patients who understand the plan are more likely to attend follow-up and engage with lifestyle advice.

"The reason I want to repeat the blood tests first, rather than going straight to a scan, is that sometimes these results go back to normal on their own — and before we do anything else, I want to make sure we know if this is an ongoing problem or a one-off."
3
Negotiate — offer something today

Even if the investigation plan is watchful waiting, always leave the patient with something concrete: a lifestyle target, a repeat blood test date, a written plan, a contact number if symptoms develop. Leaving with nothing is a missed opportunity.

"So here is what we will do today: I will arrange the additional blood tests, we will repeat the liver panel in 8 weeks, and in the meantime I would like to go through what you can do now that we know will genuinely help — can we do that together?"
Key principle: The patient with "mildly abnormal liver tests" is at a clinical crossroads — engaged now, they can prevent cirrhosis; dismissed now, they will not return until the disease is symptomatic and advanced. The consultation today is the most important intervention.

7C — Non-medication management: mechanism + evidence + tailored advice
Lifestyle intervention is the primary treatment for both MASLD and ARLD — not an optional add-on. A 5–10% reduction in body weight reduces hepatic steatosis by 40–80%, and a 10% reduction in weight can reverse early MASH. Advice must be specific, measurable, and feasible within the patient's psychosocial context. Generic "eat less and exercise more" is not a treatment plan.
⚖️
Weight Reduction
Target: 5–10% body weight loss over 6–12 months
Mechanism

Visceral adipose tissue drives hepatic steatosis via free fatty acid flux and insulin resistance. Weight loss reduces hepatic fat content, ALT, and fibrosis progression. Even 3–5% weight loss reduces steatosis significantly.

Practical

Mediterranean diet (olive oil, fish, vegetables, whole grains, limited red meat and processed food) is NICE-recommended for MASLD. Calorie deficit of 500–750 kcal/day achievable via dietary diary and dietitian referral. Referral to tier 2 or 3 weight management services if BMI >35.

10% weight loss → reversal of MASH in up to 90%
🏃
Physical Activity
150 min moderate aerobic activity per week
Mechanism

Exercise independently reduces hepatic steatosis through AMPK activation and improved insulin sensitivity, even without weight loss. Resistance training additionally reduces visceral adiposity and improves metabolic parameters.

Practical

150 minutes moderate intensity (brisk walking, cycling, swimming) per week OR 75 minutes vigorous. Add 2 sessions resistance training weekly. NHS Couch to 5K app is evidence-based for sedentary patients. Social prescribing to walking groups or leisure centre referral.

Exercise alone reduces ALT by ~25% without weight change
🍷
Alcohol Reduction / Abstinence
Complete abstinence if ARLD; ≤14 units/week if MASLD
Mechanism

Alcohol directly damages hepatocytes via oxidative stress (acetaldehyde), mitochondrial dysfunction, and endotoxin-mediated inflammation. In ARLD, abstinence can normalise LFTs and even reverse early cirrhosis. In MASLD, alcohol worsens steatohepatitis even at moderate intake levels.

Practical

AUDIT-C score first. If hazardous or harmful: brief intervention, leaflets (Drinkaware, Alcohol Change UK). If dependent: refer alcohol liaison ARLD pathway — do NOT advise abrupt cessation (seizure risk). Supported withdrawal with community or inpatient detox. Alcohol care team referral if available.

Abstinence normalises GGT in 4–6 weeks; reverses early cirrhosis in 1–2 years
🩸
Glycaemic and Metabolic Control
HbA1c <53 mmol/mol; BP <130/80; LDL-C <1.8 mmol/L if CVD risk
Mechanism

Insulin resistance is the central pathophysiological driver of MASLD. Hyperglycaemia drives hepatic gluconeogenesis and de novo lipogenesis, directly worsening steatosis and fibrosis. GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide) have specific hepatoprotective effects beyond glycaemic control.

Practical

Optimise T2DM management (metformin continues — safe in MASLD unless eGFR <30; SGLT-2i or GLP-1 RA preferred second agent as both have hepatoprotective effects). Blood pressure control with ACEi or ARB if proteinuria present. Statin for CVD risk reduction (safe in liver disease; NICE CG181).

GLP-1 RA (semaglutide) reduces MASH fibrosis by ≥1 stage in 43% (NEJM 2024)
💊
Medication Review
Remove hepatotoxic drugs; review all supplements
Mechanism

DILI accounts for up to 20% of acute liver failure cases. NSAIDs, herbal remedies, and supplements are the most commonly missed causes because patients do not volunteer this information. The RUCAM score quantifies likelihood of drug causation.

Practical

Stop all herbal supplements immediately. NSAIDs: switch to paracetamol (<2g/day if normal synthetic function) or topical NSAID. Statins: do not stop without cause — safe in liver disease and provide hepatoprotection. If DILI confirmed: Yellow Card reporting and RUCAM documentation.

DILI resolves in 80% within 8 weeks of stopping the offending drug
💉
Vaccination & Infection Prevention
Hepatitis A + B vaccination if not immune; pneumococcal + influenza annually if cirrhosis
Mechanism

Patients with chronic liver disease have impaired innate immunity — infections are disproportionately severe. Hepatitis A superimposed on chronic liver disease has mortality of up to 70%. Annual influenza vaccination halves hospitalisation risk in cirrhosis.

Practical

Check hepatitis A and B immunity status (anti-HAV IgG, anti-HBs). Vaccinate if non-immune — accelerated Hep B schedule available if urgent. Pneumococcal vaccine (PPV23) for all patients with cirrhosis. Annual influenza vaccination. Avoid live vaccines if on immunosuppression (AIH).

Hep A superinfection on chronic liver disease: 70% mortality — vaccination is essential

Antivirals for hepatitis B and C, ursodeoxycholic acid for PBC, and immunosuppression for AIH are specialist-initiated. Always review the medication list for hepatotoxic drugs before adding new ones.

  • Hepatitis B: tenofovir or entecavir — lifelong antiviral suppression; GP monitors renal function and HBV DNA annually
  • PBC: ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA) 13–15 mg/kg/day — initiated by hepatology, GP prescribes on shared care
  • AIH: prednisolone plus azathioprine — specialist initiated; GP monitors FBC and LFTs monthly
  • Haemochromatosis: therapeutic venesection — 1 unit blood every 1–2 weeks until ferritin <50 μg/L, then maintenance phlebotomy
  • Wilson's disease: D-penicillamine or trientine — specialist managed; GP monitors urine copper and FBC
Shared care protocol required before GP prescribes specialist-initiated treatments. Check local formulary.
Step 4 — Acute Decompensation Management (Hospital)

Acute liver failure: N-acetylcysteine IV (paracetamol OD), King's College criteria assessment, ICU, transplant listing consideration.

Variceal bleeding: Terlipressin IV plus OGD plus band ligation. Propranolol for secondary prevention (target HR 55–60 bpm).

Ascites: Low-sodium diet (<90 mmol/day NaCl), spironolactone 100 mg OD (titrate up), furosemide 40 mg OD if needed. Paracentesis if tense ascites. Prophylactic norfloxacin if previous SBP.

Hepatic encephalopathy: Lactulose (titrate to 2–3 soft stools/day), rifaximin 550 mg BD for recurrent episodes, dietary protein not restricted below 1.2 g/kg/day.

Special Prescribing Situations

Statins in liver disease: SAFE and beneficial in MASLD and compensated ARLD. Do NOT stop statins because of mildly raised LFTs (<3× ULN) — the risk of stopping (increased CVD mortality) outweighs the hepatic risk. Stop only if >3× ULN and attributable to statin.

Paracetamol in liver disease: Safe at <2g/day in patients with chronic liver disease with normal synthetic function. Avoid in active heavy alcohol use.

Metformin in liver disease: Continue in MASLD and compensated cirrhosis. Withhold if AKI or severe hepatic impairment (risk of lactic acidosis). Not contraindicated by liver disease alone.

NSAIDs in cirrhosis: Absolutely contraindicated — risk of AKI, GI bleeding (variceal plus NSAID-related), and hepatorenal syndrome. Use paracetamol or opioids if analgesic needed.

7G — Psychosocial impact of the diagnosis: driving, work, relationships & daily life
🤝
Living with a liver diagnosis — what the patient needs to know beyond the clinical facts
A liver diagnosis carries disproportionate social stigma — particularly when it is alcohol-related. Patients may conceal the diagnosis from family, employers, and insurers, leading to delayed treatment and poor adherence. Healthcare professionals must proactively address the real-life consequences of the diagnosis, as patients are unlikely to volunteer these concerns without prompting. Shame is one of the most powerful barriers to engagement with alcohol-related liver disease treatment.
🚘
Driving

Stable MASLD or chronic viral hepatitis without decompensation: no driving restriction, no DVLA notification required.

Cirrhosis with encephalopathy (even single episode): must stop driving and notify DVLA — encephalopathy impairs cognitive function required for safe driving.

Hepatic encephalopathy is a notifiable condition to DVLA (Group 1 and Group 2 licences). HGV/PCV licence holders with cirrhosis face more stringent DVLA criteria — refer to DVLA medical unit.

"I do need to mention driving — as long as you are feeling well and there is no confusion from the liver, you are fine to drive. But I will let you know if that ever changes."
💼
Work and Employment

MASLD and compensated chronic liver disease: no occupational restriction in most jobs. Fatigue is common and may affect productivity — acknowledge it clinically.

Certain occupations require DVLA or occupational health notification: pilots, HGV drivers, merchant seafarers, armed forces, and safety-critical roles. Hepatic encephalopathy precludes these roles.

Alcohol-related liver disease may warrant referral to occupational health if there is a workplace drinking culture or if the patient needs time off for treatment.

"Your liver condition should not affect your ability to work for now — but if you need a sick note or a letter for occupational health, please let us know."
💑
Relationships and Disclosure

Hepatitis B and C are transmissible — partner notification is a clinical and public health duty. Sexual partners and household contacts require testing and vaccination (Hep B).

The diagnosis of liver disease — particularly if alcohol-related — carries significant relationship impact. Partners may feel deceived by concealed drinking; the diagnosis can be a crisis point or a catalyst for change.

Offer to involve partners (with consent) in the discussion of the diagnosis and management plan — this significantly improves ARLD treatment engagement.

"Is there someone close to you who you would like to be involved in understanding the diagnosis? Sometimes it helps to have a second pair of ears — and for hepatitis, we do need to check your family members as well."
🏋
Physical Activity and Daily Life

Exercise is beneficial and encouraged in MASLD, ARLD (compensated), and chronic viral hepatitis. No restriction on activity in compensated liver disease.

Contact sports should be avoided in patients with known portal hypertension (splenomegaly) — risk of splenic rupture from blunt abdominal trauma is increased.

Fatigue is a common and underrecognised symptom of chronic liver disease (especially PBC) — validate it, as patients often feel dismissed when they describe fatigue with normal-looking tests.

"The fatigue you are feeling is real and is a known part of liver disease — it is not 'just stress.' We can look at ways to manage it and refer you if it is significantly affecting your quality of life."
🍺
Alcohol and Social Life

For ARLD patients: complete abstinence is the goal, not harm reduction. Even moderate alcohol in established liver disease significantly worsens fibrosis. This must be communicated clearly but without shame.

For MASLD without ARLD: the safe alcohol threshold is <14 units/week (NHS) — alcohol worsens steatohepatitis even at moderate intake levels due to additive hepatotoxic effects.

Social life disruption from abstinence is a significant barrier — refer to alcohol liaison, ARLD peer support groups, and Alcoholics Anonymous if the patient is willing.

"I know asking you to stop drinking completely is a big ask — and I want to help you work out how to do that in a way that fits your life. Can we talk about what support might help?"
🛡️
Insurance and Legal Implications

Life insurance and critical illness policies: liver disease (especially cirrhosis) may affect premium costs or exclusions. Patients should seek financial advice but are not required to retrospectively disclose to existing policies — only new policies require declaration.

Travel insurance: declare liver disease, particularly if cirrhosis or on specialist treatment. Failure to declare may invalidate cover.

Alcohol-related liver disease and employment: the Equality Act 2010 does not protect alcohol dependence itself, but associated mental health conditions may require reasonable adjustment.

"I would encourage you to check with your travel and life insurers about your liver condition — it is better to declare it and know where you stand than to find out later there is a problem with cover."

7J — Safety-netting: exact phrases + medico-legal rationale

⚠ Three scenario-specific phrases — use these verbatim

🔴 Emergency — acute decompensation
"If you notice your skin or eyes turning yellow, if you become confused or very drowsy, if you vomit blood or your stools turn very dark, or if your abdomen swells up rapidly — these are serious warning signs and you should call 999 or go to A&E immediately without waiting to see us."
This phrase covers all five major complications of decompensated liver disease: jaundice (liver failure), encephalopathy (confusion), variceal bleeding (haematemesis/melaena), and ascites (abdominal swelling). Medicolegally, naming specific symptoms protects the GP from a claim of inadequate safety-netting if the patient presents late with a preventable complication. Vague "if you feel unwell" is legally insufficient.
💊 Medication — drug-induced liver injury or new drug concern
"While we are investigating the liver results, please do not take any new supplements, herbal remedies, or over-the-counter products without checking with us first — some of these can worsen the liver problem significantly. If you are prescribed anything new by another doctor or dentist, please mention the liver tests to them."
DILI from herbal supplements and OTC products is systematically under-recognised. Patients do not consider supplements as "medication" and do not volunteer this information. Specifically naming "supplements" and "herbal remedies" is more effective than "medication" alone. Pre-warning protects the patient from inadvertent DILI during the investigation period and documents that the GP discharged their duty of care regarding polypharmacy risk.
🟠 Alcohol — specific if ARLD suspected or confirmed
"I want to be honest with you about the alcohol — continuing to drink at current levels while we investigate the liver will make the tests harder to interpret and may make the situation worse. I would recommend aiming for complete abstinence during the 8-week investigation period. If you are worried about stopping suddenly, please tell me — it is not safe to stop abruptly if you are drinking heavily, and we can help you do this safely."
This phrase does two things: it provides clear specific honest advice about alcohol reduction (not vague "cutting down") and it explicitly warns about withdrawal risk from abrupt cessation in dependent drinkers — a medicolegally critical safety point. Alcohol withdrawal seizures and Wernicke's encephalopathy are preventable harms that the GP must address. Documenting this conversation protects the practitioner and creates an engagement opportunity.

🚦 1 — Triage System
Abnormal LFT result → Is the patient well or unwell? → Degree of elevation? → Features of decompensation?
↓
🔴 Emergency — 999 / A&E now
  • Jaundice plus confusion or asterixis (hepatic encephalopathy)
  • Haematemesis or melaena with known or suspected liver disease (varices)
  • Charcot's triad: fever plus jaundice plus RUQ pain (ascending cholangitis)
  • Acute liver failure: INR >1.5 plus encephalopathy (any cause)
  • Paracetamol OD with raised LFTs (NAC within 8–10 hours)
999 / A&E immediately
🟠 Urgent — same-day or 2WW
  • ALT/AST >10× ULN in well patient — same-day GP review
  • New clinical jaundice without encephalopathy — same-day
  • New ascites with raised LFTs — same-day referral
  • Weight loss plus raised ALP or abdominal mass — 2WW cancer
  • INR >1.2 (not anticoagulated) — same-day review
Same-day review / 2WW referral
🟢 Routine — GP primary care
  • ALT/AST <3× ULN, well patient, likely metabolic
  • Isolated raised GGT — alcohol or medications
  • Isolated raised ALP — check GGT (bone vs liver)
  • MASLD on USS, FIB-4 <1.3, no decompensation
Repeat LFTs 8 weeks, lifestyle, FIB-4
🔬 2 — Pattern Recognition & Diagnostic Pathway
LFT Pattern → Likely Cause
Hepatocellular (ALT dominant): MASLD, ARLD (AST:ALT >2), viral hepatitis, DILI, AIH, ischaemic
Cholestatic (ALP plus GGT dominant): gallstones/CBD obstruction, PBC, PSC, drugs, pancreatic Ca, biliary stricture
Mixed: DILI (many drugs), infiltrative disease, ARLD
Isolated GGT: alcohol, enzyme inducers, fatty liver, obesity
Isolated ALP (GGT normal): bone origin — Paget's, bony mets, pregnancy
Staged Investigation Approach
1st line (all): Repeat LFTs 8 weeks + FBC, Hep B/C serology, fasting glucose/HbA1c, lipids, coagulation
FIB-4: Calculate if MASLD or ARLD — <1.3 GP manage, 1.3–2.67 ELF test, >2.67 hepatology
USS abdomen: If LFTs persist beyond 8 weeks or cholestatic pattern
Autoimmune screen: ANA, ASMA, AMA, anti-LKM1, IgG/IgM if hepatocellular without clear aetiology
Iron studies: Ferritin + transferrin saturation if haemochromatosis suspected

💊 4 — Medication Decision & Choice
Aetiology-Specific Drug Choice
MASLD + T2DM: GLP-1 RA (semaglutide — NICE TA 2024 for MASH) + SGLT-2i
MASLD non-diabetic: Pioglitazone (MASH confirmed) or Vitamin E 800 IU
PBC: UDCA 13–15 mg/kg/day (specialist initiated)
AIH: Prednisolone + azathioprine (specialist initiated)
Hep C: Direct-acting antivirals — 8–12 weeks, >95% cure
Hep B: Tenofovir or entecavir (lifelong — specialist)
Haemochromatosis: Therapeutic venesection (specialist)
Ascites (cirrhosis): Spironolactone 100 mg OD + low-sodium diet
Key Prescribing Rules
✓ Statins: SAFE in MASLD and compensated ARLD — do NOT stop for LFT elevation <3× ULN
✓ Metformin: Continue in MASLD and compensated cirrhosis (hold only if AKI or eGFR <30)
✓ Paracetamol: Safe <2g/day in chronic liver disease with normal synthetic function
✗ NSAIDs in cirrhosis: ABSOLUTELY CONTRAINDICATED — AKI, GI bleed, hepatorenal syndrome
✗ UDCA in primary care: Do NOT start without confirmed PBC diagnosis from hepatology
✗ Herbal supplements: Stop ALL during LFT investigation — frequent unrecognised DILI cause
⚠ 5 — Safety Netting & Follow-Up
🔴 Emergency — decompensation warning
"If you notice yellowing of your skin or eyes, become confused or very drowsy, vomit blood, or your abdomen swells — call 999 or go to A&E immediately."
💊 Medication — supplements and drugs
"Do not take any new supplements, herbal remedies, or over-the-counter products without checking with us first — some can worsen the liver problem significantly."
🟠 Alcohol — withdrawal risk warning
"If you decide to stop drinking, do not stop abruptly — please tell us first so we can help you do it safely."
Follow-up timeline
1
8 weeks: Repeat LFTs + second-line investigation results review + FIB-4 calculation
2
3 months: USS results review + fibrosis stratification + referral decision if FIB-4 >2.67
3
6 months: Metabolic reassessment (HbA1c, lipids, BP, weight) + pharmacotherapy review
4
12 months: Annual LFT + FIB-4 + USS (ARLD/MASLD) + HCC surveillance if cirrhosis
5
Ongoing: Shared care with hepatology if advanced disease
📌 Add to chronic disease register — patients not on a recall list are lost to follow-up

💬 Key Phrases — ICE, Diagnosis & Plan
Ideas
"When you found out the liver tests were abnormal, what did you think might be causing it?"
Concerns
"What is your biggest worry about this result? A lot of people worry it could be something serious — is that something that has crossed your mind?"
Expectations
"What were you hoping we might do today, or what were you expecting might happen next?"
Validate (cancer fear)
"That must make this result feel much more frightening — your father's story is completely understandable context for why you are worried. Let me explain what we are looking at."
Explain (plain language)
"Think of your liver like a processing factory — the tests suggest the factory is under strain, not broken. We have found this early, when we can still do something about it."
Close
"To summarise — we will repeat the blood tests in 8 weeks, add hepatitis and iron tests, and in the meantime the most important thing is reducing the ibuprofen and cutting back on alcohol. Does that all make sense? Is there anything else on your mind?"
🚫 9 Danger Zones — Instant Deductions
✗
Asking for results the GP already has→ Use existing information: "I can see from the notes that your ALT is 87 — can I ask what you know about what that means?"
✗
Not asking about alcohol when LFTs are raised→ Always ask, always quantify in units, always do so non-judgementally — "I ask everyone about this"
✗
Not asking about herbal supplements and OTC products→ "Medications" alone is insufficient — ask specifically about supplements, herbals, and anything bought online
✗
Vague safety-netting ("come back if worse")→ Name specific symptoms: jaundice, confusion, haematemesis, abdominal swelling — call 999, not the surgery
✗
Not addressing the cancer concern when signalled→ Name it: "I think you might be worried this could be something like cancer — let me address that directly"
✗
Starting UDCA or corticosteroids in primary care without confirmed diagnosis→ "Once the specialist confirms the diagnosis, they will start the appropriate treatment and we will monitor you here"
✗
Stopping statins for mild LFT elevation→ Statins are safe in liver disease; stopping increases CVD mortality. Continue unless >3× ULN and attributable to statin.
✗
Advising abrupt cessation of alcohol without warning about withdrawal→ "If you are drinking heavily, stopping suddenly can be dangerous — please let us help you do this safely to avoid withdrawal"
✗
Not mentioning FIB-4 in MASLD or ARLD→ FIB-4 is the NICE-recommended non-invasive fibrosis test in primary care — naming it demonstrates current clinical knowledge and scores Tasks marks
💊 Drug Quick-Pick by Scenario
MASLD plus T2DM (BMI >27)
→Semaglutide 2.4 mg SC/wk
Weight loss is first-line; GLP-1 has off-label MASH benefit. Add SGLT-2i (empagliflozin) in T2DM for cardiorenal protection. Resmetirom is the first MASH-specific agent (not yet routine UK/NICE).
MASLD without T2DM (MASH confirmed)
→Pioglitazone 30–45 mg OD
Contraindicated in heart failure. Warn about weight gain and bone fracture risk. Requires histological confirmation ideally.
Cirrhosis with ascites
→Spironolactone 100 mg OD
Add furosemide 40 mg if insufficient. Low-sodium diet (<2g/day). Monitor U&E weekly initially. NSAIDs absolutely contraindicated.
Hepatitis C (any genotype)
→Sofosbuvir/velpatasvir 8–12 wks
>95% cure rate. Check drug interactions (BNF). HCV RNA at end of treatment plus 12 weeks post = SVR12 (cure).
Primary Biliary Cholangitis (PBC confirmed)
→UDCA 13–15 mg/kg/day
Specialist-initiated only. GP prescribes on shared care. Check ALP reduction >25% at 12 months (biochemical response).
Drug-induced liver injury (DILI confirmed)
→Stop offending drug immediately
RUCAM causality assessment. Yellow Card reporting. Recheck LFTs in 1 week. If >3× ULN persists after 4 weeks → hepatology phone advice. No rechallenge without specialist input.
